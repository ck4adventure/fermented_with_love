# A basic sourdough loaf

This recipe makes a single 1lb loaf — a manageable size for a first bake or a quiet week of sandwiches. The flour blend is 90% bread flour and 10% whole wheat, which gives you structure and a mild, wheaty depth without making the dough difficult to handle. Hydration is 75%, which produces an open, slightly chewy crumb.

The whole process spans about 24 hours, but most of that is hands-off waiting.

## The numbers

**Total dough weight:** ~500g (yields approximately a 1lb finished loaf after baking off moisture)

| Ingredient | Weight | Baker's % |
|---|---|---|
| Bread flour | 225g | 90% |
| Whole wheat flour | 25g | 10% |
| Water | 187g | 75% |
| Active starter | 50g | 20% |
| Salt | 9g | 3.6% |

**Total flour:** 250g  
**Total dough weight:** ~471g

## Before you start

Your starter needs to be active and at or near its peak — doubled in size, domed on top, bubbly throughout. If you refrigerate your starter, take it out the night before, feed it, and let it rise overnight. Use it in the morning when it's at its liveliest.

Baking sourdough in a Dutch oven gives you the best results at home. The enclosed space traps steam during the first half of the bake, which lets the crust stay soft long enough for the loaf to spring up before it sets. A cast iron Dutch oven works well; so does any oven-safe pot with a tight-fitting lid.

## Method

### 1. Mix the dough (Day 1, morning)

Combine the bread flour and whole wheat flour in a large bowl. In a separate container, mix the starter into the water until distributed. Pour the liquid into the flour and stir until no dry flour remains. It will look shaggy and rough. Cover the bowl and let it rest for **30–60 minutes**. This rest period (called autolyse) lets the flour hydrate fully and the gluten begin to develop before you add salt.

After the rest, add the salt. Squeeze it into the dough with your fingers until it's fully incorporated, then fold the dough over itself several times in the bowl. It will feel sticky — that's expected at this hydration.

### 2. Bulk fermentation with stretch and fold (3–5 hours)

Cover the bowl and leave it at room temperature. This is bulk fermentation — the long rise where flavor develops.

During the first two hours, perform **stretch and folds** every 30 minutes. To do one: wet your hand slightly, grab one side of the dough, stretch it upward until you feel resistance, then fold it back over the center. Rotate the bowl 90° and repeat, working your way around all four sides. That's one set. Do four to six sets total over the first couple of hours, then leave the dough alone.

Bulk fermentation is done when the dough has grown by about 50–75%, feels airy and jiggly when you shake the bowl gently, and has a slightly domed surface. The exact time depends on your room temperature and starter activity — cooler kitchens take longer, warmer ones move faster. Trust the look and feel over the clock.

### 3. Shape

Turn the dough out onto an unfloured surface. Let it rest for 20 minutes to relax.

For a round loaf (boule): Use a bench scraper or your hands to gently stretch the dough into a rough rectangle, then fold the sides in toward the center, then roll the top down toward you, creating surface tension as you go. Flip it seam-side down and use the bench scraper to drag it toward you across the surface a few times — this tightens the skin.

For an oval loaf (batard): Similar process, but elongate the shape before rolling.

You're looking for a smooth, taut surface. The tension you build here is what holds the loaf's shape during the final rise.

### 4. Cold fermentation overnight

Dust a banneton (proofing basket) generously with rice flour, or line a bowl with a well-floured cloth. Place the dough in seam-side up, cover, and refrigerate overnight — **8–14 hours**. This cold proof develops flavor, makes scoring easier, and firms up the loaf so it holds its shape going into a hot oven.

### 5. Bake (Day 2, morning)

Place your Dutch oven in the oven and preheat to **500°F (260°C)** for at least 45 minutes. You want the pot genuinely hot before the dough goes in.

Take the dough out of the fridge right before baking — don't let it warm up first.

Cut a piece of parchment paper large enough to lower the dough into the pot as a sling. Flip the dough out onto the parchment seam-side down. Score the top with a sharp knife or lame — a single decisive slash at a 30–45° angle, about ½ inch deep, is enough. Scoring lets the loaf expand in a controlled direction; without it, it tears unpredictably.

Lower the dough into the hot Dutch oven using the parchment, cover with the lid, and bake for **20 minutes**. Remove the lid and reduce the temperature to **450°F (230°C)**. Continue baking for another **20–25 minutes** until the crust is deep brown — darker than you think you want. An internal temperature of 205–210°F confirms it's done.

### 6. Cool completely

This is the hardest part. Transfer the loaf to a wire rack and leave it alone for **at least one hour**, preferably two. The interior is still cooking via residual heat, and the crumb structure is setting. Cutting into it too early gives you a gummy, undercooked interior even if the crust looks perfect.

## Adjusting as you go

**Dense crumb, not much oven spring:** The starter may not have been active enough, or bulk fermentation was cut short. Let the starter peak more fully before using it, and give the dough more time in bulk.

**Flat loaf that spread sideways:** Too long in bulk fermentation, or shaping didn't build enough tension. Watch for over-proofing signs (very gassy, tears easily when handled) and focus on the drag-and-tension step during shaping.

**Gummy interior:** Needs more baking time or was cut too soon. Add 5 minutes to the uncovered bake, or let it cool longer.

**Pale crust:** Go darker. The sugars and proteins in the crust need high heat to caramelize properly, and most home bakers pull their loaves too early.
