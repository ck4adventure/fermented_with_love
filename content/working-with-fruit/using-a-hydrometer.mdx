# Using a hydrometer

A hydrometer looks a little like a glass thermometer with a weighted bulb at the bottom — and the first time you hold one, it's not immediately obvious what you're supposed to do with it. Once you understand what it's measuring, though, it becomes one of the most useful tools you'll use.

## What a hydrometer actually measures

A hydrometer measures the **specific gravity** (SG) of a liquid — its density relative to pure water. Water has a specific gravity of 1.000. Dissolve sugar in water and the liquid becomes denser, so the reading goes up: a sugary fruit must might read 1.080 or 1.090. Alcohol is *less* dense than water, so as yeast converts sugar to alcohol during fermentation, the reading drops.

This gives you a window into what's happening inside your ferment without having to guess. Two readings taken at different points in time tell you:

- How much sugar you started with (your **Original Gravity**, or OG)
- How much sugar is left when fermentation finishes (your **Final Gravity**, or FG)
- From those two numbers, how much alcohol your wine contains

## Calculating ABV

The formula is straightforward:

```
(OG − FG) × 131.25 = ABV%
```

So if your must started at 1.085 and finished at 1.010:

```
(1.085 − 1.010) × 131.25 = 9.8% ABV
```

That's a solid fruit wine — stable enough to store for a few months, with good flavor potential.

## What you need

- Your hydrometer
- A **trial jar** — a tall narrow tube, usually plastic or glass, that the hydrometer can float freely in. These are inexpensive at any homebrew shop. In a pinch, a tall glass works, but it's fiddly.
- A **wine thief** or turkey baster to draw a sample from your fermentation vessel without disturbing the whole batch

## How to take a reading

**Step 1 — Draw a sample.** Use your wine thief or turkey baster to pull enough liquid to fill the trial jar about three-quarters full. Always sample from the liquid, not the fruit cap if you're fermenting on pulp. Don't put the hydrometer directly into your fermentation vessel — you need the hydrometer to float freely, and you don't want to risk breaking it in your batch.

**Step 2 — Drop the hydrometer in.** Lower it gently into the trial jar and let it float. Give it a light spin between your fingers to dislodge any bubbles clinging to the stem — bubbles act as tiny flotation devices and will push the reading higher than it should be.

**Step 3 — Read at the meniscus.** This is the part that trips people up at first. Where the liquid meets the hydrometer stem, it curves slightly upward — that curved surface is called the **meniscus**. You want to read the number at the *bottom* of the curve, where the flat surface of the liquid is, not where it climbs up the glass. Crouch down so your eye is level with the liquid surface to see this correctly. Reading from above will give you a lower number than the actual gravity.

**Step 4 — Note the temperature.** Hydrometers are calibrated to a specific temperature — usually 60°F (15.5°C) or 68°F (20°C), printed somewhere on the stem or the packaging. If your liquid is significantly warmer or colder than that, your reading will be slightly off. For most home fermentation purposes this error is small enough to ignore, but if you're being precise, correction charts are easy to find online.

**Step 5 — Handle the sample carefully.** This is a judgment call. If fermentation is well underway and your sample was drawn cleanly, many people return it to the vessel. For wild ferments especially, I tend to discard the sample or drink it rather than reintroduce liquid that's been exposed to air and handled outside the vessel.

## Reading the scale

Most hydrometers have multiple scales printed on the stem — this can look confusing at first. The one you want is **Specific Gravity**, showing values like 1.000, 1.010, 1.020, and so on. You may also see a **Brix** scale (used commercially in winemaking, measures sugar as a percentage) and a **Potential Alcohol** scale (a pre-calculated shortcut). They're all measuring the same thing in different units. Stick with SG until you're comfortable, then explore the others if you're curious.

## When to take readings

**Before fermentation begins (OG):** Ideally you take this reading before you add sugar and before visible fermentation has started. If you're doing a wild ferment that started unexpectedly quickly — which happens — you can still take a reading early in fermentation, just know it won't be quite as accurate as a true pre-fermentation reading.

**During fermentation:** Optional, but useful. A mid-fermentation reading tells you how far along things are and can warn you if fermentation has stalled unexpectedly.

**At the end (FG):** Take this reading when bubbling has slowed dramatically or stopped. Then take it again two days later. If the reading is the same both times, fermentation is complete. A single stopped-bubbling reading isn't enough — CO2 can stop being visible before all the sugar is gone.

## What the numbers look like in practice

Here are some rough reference points to build intuition:

| Reading | What it means |
|---|---|
| 1.080–1.100 | Typical starting gravity for a fruit wine aiming for 11–13% ABV |
| 1.050–1.070 | Apple juice range; cider territory without sugar additions |
| 1.040–1.060 | Plum or stone fruit without additions; likely needs sugar to reach stability |
| 1.010–1.020 | Fermentation winding down; wine still has residual sweetness |
| 1.000–1.005 | Dry to nearly dry; most sugar has been converted |
| Below 1.000 | Bone dry; alcohol content is now making the liquid lighter than water |

## A note on wild ferments and no OG reading

If your wild ferment took off before you had a chance to take an OG reading — or if you're working from fruit that was already partially processing when you got the hydrometer — don't worry. A current reading still tells you where you are now, and you can make informed decisions about whether to add sugar, how close to done the ferment might be, and roughly what ABV range you're in. It's not perfect, but it's far better than guessing entirely.
