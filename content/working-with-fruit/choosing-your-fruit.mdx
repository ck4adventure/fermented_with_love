# Choosing your fruit

Every decision you make about fruit — where it came from, how ripe it is, how it was grown — shapes what you're able to make from it. This isn't about being precious or requiring perfect ingredients. It's about understanding what you're working with so you can work with it intelligently.

Good fruit wine starts with fruit you'd want to eat. That's the baseline. From there, a few other considerations come into play.

## Ripeness and what it changes

Ripeness is probably the single most important variable in fruit selection, and it affects your wine in several ways at once.

**Sugar content rises with ripeness.** As fruit ripens, starches convert to sugars. A barely-ripe plum and a fully ripe plum from the same tree can have meaningfully different sugar contents — the riper fruit will give you a higher starting gravity and need less added sugar to reach your alcohol target. Very overripe fruit (on the edge of fermentation already) can be even higher in sugar, though it comes with tradeoffs.

**Flavor compounds develop with ripeness.** The aromatic compounds that make fruit taste of itself — the thing you're ultimately trying to capture in the wine — peak at full ripeness and begin to break down as fruit goes past it. Underripe fruit tends to produce sharp, thin wines with green or astringent notes. Fully ripe fruit produces wines with depth and genuine fruit character. Overripe or slightly fermented fruit can add complexity but also funk and earthiness that may or may not be what you're after.

**Wild yeast populations peak around full ripeness.** The bloom on a ripe plum or grape — that faint dusty coating — is largely yeast. It's most abundant on fully ripe fruit that's had time to develop its natural microbial community. Underripe fruit picked early has had less time to accumulate wild yeast. This matters directly for wild fermentation: the best wild yeast populations live on the best, ripest fruit.

The sweet spot for wine fruit is fully ripe to just past peak — when the fruit is as flavorful as it's going to get, before decay sets in. If you're working with fruit you've foraged or grown yourself and can pick at exactly the right moment, you have an advantage commercial winemakers pay significant money to replicate.

## Where your fruit comes from

This is where wild fermentation diverges most sharply from conventional winemaking, and it's worth understanding why.

**Foraged and garden fruit** is the gold standard for wild fermentation. Fruit from a neighbor's backyard plum tree, foraged from a park or roadside, or grown in your own garden hasn't been treated with fungicides, hasn't spent days in refrigerated transit, and has been developing its wild yeast population in your specific environment. The wine it produces carries something of that place — the microorganisms living in your neighborhood, on those particular trees, in this particular season. This is what people mean when they talk about terroir in wine, and it's genuinely real. You can taste the difference.

**Farmers market fruit**, particularly from small organic or low-spray farms, is the next best option. Ask the farmer directly whether they spray fungicides — many small farms don't, or use them minimally and not close to harvest. Farmers market fruit is also typically harvested closer to full ripeness than supermarket fruit, which spends more time in transit and is often picked underripe to survive it.

**Supermarket fruit** is the hardest case for wild fermentation. Commercially grown fruit is routinely treated with fungicides specifically designed to eliminate surface mold and yeast — the same organisms you're counting on to ferment your wine. The fruit may look beautiful and taste fine fresh, but its wild yeast population is likely sparse or nonexistent. Wild fermentations started with heavily treated supermarket fruit often struggle to get going, stall early, or produce less interesting results than the same fruit from a better source would.

This doesn't mean supermarket fruit is useless for winemaking — it's fine if you're planning to add commercial yeast. But if wild fermentation is the goal, sourcing matters more than most beginners expect.

## Organic vs conventional

Organic certification is a reasonable proxy for lower fungicide use, but it's not a guarantee and it's not the only path to good wild-fermentation fruit. Some conventionally grown fruit from small farms has excellent wild yeast populations because the farmer doesn't spray heavily or doesn't spray close to harvest. Some certified organic fruit has been treated with approved organic fungicides that still affect surface microorganisms.

The more useful question than "is it organic?" is "how was this fruit grown and when was it last treated?" A direct conversation with a farmer at a market will tell you more than the certification sticker.

## Thinking about sugar content by fruit

Different fruits start with different natural sugar levels, which affects how much you'll need to add to reach your fermentation target. Here are rough ranges to build intuition from — individual fruits vary based on variety, growing conditions, and ripeness, so treat these as starting points rather than fixed values:

**High natural sugar (often need little or no addition for a modest ABV):**
Grapes (1.070–1.100+), ripe figs (1.065–1.080), very ripe stone fruit at peak season.

**Moderate natural sugar (typically need additions to reach 11–12% ABV):**
Apples and pears (1.045–1.065), plums (1.045–1.060, though sorbitol inflates the apparent sweetness — see the sugar additions page), cherries (1.055–1.075), ripe peaches and nectarines.

**Lower natural sugar (usually need meaningful additions):**
Berries of most kinds (1.040–1.060), citrus juice (1.040–1.055), rhubarb (very low, needs significant sugar addition and benefits from blending with higher-sugar fruit).

These numbers are why a hydrometer is so useful — rather than guessing where your particular batch of fruit falls, you measure it, and you know exactly what you're working with.

## Fruit condition

Whatever you're working with, go through it before you process it. Remove anything with visible mold — not just the moldy piece but any fruit it was in contact with. A small soft spot you can cut around is fine; actual mold colonies introduce organisms you don't want and can affect the flavor of an entire batch.

Slightly overripe fruit that's just beginning to show fermentation (a faintly boozy smell, beginning to soften) isn't necessarily a problem — it can actually contribute interesting complexity and carries a lively wild yeast population. Genuinely rotting fruit is a different matter and should be excluded.

The general rule: if you wouldn't eat it, don't ferment it. The fermentation process amplifies what's in the fruit, good and bad. Starting with the best fruit you have access to gives you the best foundation for everything that follows.
