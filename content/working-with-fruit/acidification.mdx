# Acidification

Acidity is one of those wine characteristics that's easy to overlook until it's wrong. When it's right, you don't think about it — the wine tastes lively and complete and you just enjoy it. When it's too low, the wine tastes flat, heavy, and somehow less of itself, like the fruit character has nowhere to stand. When it's too high, everything puckers and the wine feels aggressive.

Learning to taste for acid balance is one of the more useful skills you can develop as a winemaker, and it's more accessible than it sounds. You already have the equipment — your palate has been detecting acidity since the first time you bit into a lemon.

## What acidity actually does in wine

Acidity serves several functions simultaneously, which is why getting it right matters for more than just flavor.

It preserves the wine. Acids are hostile to many spoilage organisms, and a wine with good natural acidity is more stable during fermentation and aging than one that's low in acid. This is part of why citrus wines are naturally shelf-stable and why low-acid wines (made from very ripe or bland fruit) can be more vulnerable to problems.

It lifts and defines flavor. The acidity in a wine acts as a kind of frame for the fruit character — it makes the flavors taste more distinct and vivid. A plum wine with good acidity tastes more of plum than the same wine with low acidity. The acid isn't competing with the fruit; it's making the fruit easier to perceive.

It balances sweetness. Sweetness and acidity counterbalance each other on the palate. A wine that seems too sweet will often taste better with a small acid addition, because the acid brings the other flavors into proportion. This is why lemonade needs so much sugar — the citric acid is so strong that you need a lot of sweetness to balance it.

It gives a wine its finish. The sensation that lingers after you swallow is largely acidity. A wine with good acidity has a clean, bright finish. A wine low in acid has a finish that feels flat or short.

## How to taste for acidity

The physical sensation of acidity is unmistakable once you know what you're feeling for. It registers along the sides of the tongue and under the jaw, and it causes salivation — your mouth waters in response to sour stimuli. This is why acidic foods make you salivate and why a high-acid wine feels refreshing rather than cloying.

When you taste your wine to assess acidity, pay attention to:

**Where do you feel it?** The sides of the tongue and the back of the jaw are the primary locations. If you don't feel much there, the wine is probably low in acid.

**Does it make you salivate?** A wine with good acidity will make your mouth water. A wine low in acid won't, and it will leave a slightly heavy or coating sensation instead.

**Does it taste complete?** This is harder to describe but becomes intuitive with practice. A wine with the right acid level tastes like a finished thing. A wine low in acid tastes like it's missing something, even if you can't immediately identify what.

**How does it finish?** Swallow a small sip and wait. Good acidity leaves a clean, lingering brightness. Low acidity leaves a heavy or short finish.

A useful comparison: taste your wine alongside something with known, obvious acidity — a squeeze of lemon juice diluted in water, or a high-acid wine you already know. Comparing them directly trains your palate faster than tasting in isolation.

## The main acids in fruit wine

Different fruits contain different acids in different proportions, and they taste different from each other.

**Malic acid** is the dominant acid in apples and is present in many other fruits including grapes, cherries, and plums. It's the acid that makes green apples taste sharp — a slightly harsh, "green" quality that softens noticeably with ripeness. It can also be consumed by bacteria during malolactic fermentation (which converts it to the softer lactic acid), which is why wines that go through MLF taste rounder and less sharp.

**Tartaric acid** is most associated with grapes but found in smaller amounts in other fruit. It's the sharpest and most stable of the wine acids — it doesn't participate in malolactic fermentation and doesn't break down easily. Commercial acid blend contains tartaric acid; added in small amounts, it increases acidity in a clean, direct way without the green quality of malic acid.

**Citric acid** is the dominant acid in citrus fruit and present in smaller amounts in berries and other fruit. It's the most familiar-tasting of the three — when you think of "sour," you're probably thinking of citric acid. It's the least stable of the wine acids and can be metabolized by bacteria during fermentation, sometimes producing off-flavors in large amounts. In small additions to correct a low-acid must, it works well; as the primary acid in a wine, it can taste a bit artificial or candy-like compared to tartaric or malic.

**Commercial acid blend** is a mixture of all three in proportions designed to approximate grape wine acidity. It's a practical choice when you want to increase acidity without overthinking which individual acid to use. For most fruit wine purposes, acid blend works well and requires less decision-making than choosing individual acids.

## When fruit wine needs acid adjustment

Not all fruit wines need acidification — many fruits provide enough natural acidity on their own. Citrus wines are almost never low in acid. Berry wines are usually well-acidified. Stone fruit varies by variety and ripeness.

The wines most likely to need acid additions are those made from very ripe or overripe fruit (ripeness reduces acidity as sugars rise), low-acid fruit varieties, or blended musts where a high-acid fruit would help balance a low-acid one.

Signs a must or wine may be low in acid:
- It tastes flat, sweet, or heavy without the brightness you'd expect from the fruit
- The finish is short and coating rather than clean and refreshing
- The fruit character tastes muted even though the wine smells good

If you taste your must before fermentation and it strikes you as bland or overly sweet without a bright edge, a small acid addition before fermentation begins can transform the finished wine.

## How to add acid

The practical approach is to add in small increments and taste as you go. It's much easier to add acid than to remove it — once you've over-acidified a wine, your options are limited (blending with a low-acid wine, or waiting and hoping it softens with age).

Start with a small amount — a quarter teaspoon of acid blend per gallon dissolved in a little warm water — stir it in thoroughly, then taste. If the wine still tastes flat, add another small increment. Give it a few minutes between additions to let the acid integrate fully before you taste again.

The goal is not a perceptible sour taste — it's a lift, a brightness, a sense of completion that wasn't there before. You're not adding sourness; you're restoring balance.

## pH strips and meters

pH is the measurement that underlies acidity — it measures the concentration of hydrogen ions in a solution on a scale from 0 (most acidic) to 14 (most alkaline), with 7 being neutral. Most fruit wines ferment and taste best in the 3.0–3.5 range.

You don't need to measure pH to make good wine. Many excellent winemakers work almost entirely by taste. But pH measurement is useful when your palate is still developing and you want a number to orient to, or when you're trying to understand why two otherwise similar batches tasted different.

**pH strips** are the simplest option — inexpensive paper strips that change color in contact with a liquid, which you compare to a reference chart. They're accurate to roughly plus or minus 0.2–0.3 pH units, which is enough for a general sense of whether you're in the right range. Wine-specific pH strips calibrated for the 2.8–4.0 range are more useful than general-purpose strips.

**Digital pH meters** are more accurate and give you a specific number rather than a color comparison. They require calibration with buffer solutions (included when you buy a meter) and occasional recalibration over time. More expensive than strips but worth it if you find yourself wanting precision, or if you're making multiple batches and want to track pH as a data point across them.

Either way, think of pH measurement as supplementary information rather than the primary guide. The primary guide is always the wine itself, tasted honestly — because ultimately the goal isn't a particular number. It's a wine that tastes alive.
