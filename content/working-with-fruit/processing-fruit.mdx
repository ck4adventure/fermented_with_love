# Processing fruit

The window between fruit leaving the tree and must going into the fermentation vessel is where a lot of wild fermentation either succeeds or quietly gets undermined. The decisions you make during processing — how you wash, crush, and handle the fruit — directly affect the yeast population you're starting with.

This is worth paying attention to even before you think about sugar or equipment.

## What you're trying to protect

Wild yeast lives on the surface of fruit. You can see evidence of it on plums and grapes as a faint whitish bloom — that dusty coating is largely yeast and other microorganisms. You can't see it on most other fruit, but it's there.

Your goal during processing is to break the fruit down into juice and pulp (your **must**) while disturbing that surface population as little as possible. Anything that strips, kills, or dilutes the surface of the fruit before fermentation begins is working against you.

## To wash or not to wash

This is the question that comes up most often, and the honest answer is: it depends on the fruit and where it came from.

Fruit from a garden, a neighbor's tree, a farmers market, or foraged from a known-clean environment generally doesn't need washing. The surface is exactly where you want it to be. If there's visible dirt or debris, a very brief rinse with cold plain water is fine — but don't soak, don't scrub, and don't use anything with soap or sanitizer.

Supermarket fruit is a harder case. Commercially grown fruit is often treated with fungicides specifically designed to kill surface mold and yeast — which means the wild yeast population may be depleted or nonexistent regardless of how carefully you handle it. If you're using supermarket fruit and finding your wild ferments sluggish or stuck, this is likely why. It's not a reason to avoid it entirely, but it's worth knowing that wild fermentation works best with fruit that hasn't been through industrial agriculture.

If you do need to wash fruit, rinse it quickly, pat it dry, and work with it immediately rather than letting it sit wet.

## Removing damaged or moldy fruit

Before you process anything, go through your fruit and remove anything that's visibly moldy, rotting, or significantly damaged. A small soft spot you can cut around is fine. Actual mold colonies are not — they introduce organisms you don't want, and one badly moldy fruit can affect the flavor of an entire batch.

This isn't about being precious. It's about starting with the best raw material you have, because everything downstream follows from that.

## Crushing and breaking down the fruit

How you break down the fruit depends on what you're working with.

**Soft fruit** (plums, peaches, berries): these can often be crushed by hand or with a potato masher directly in your fermentation vessel. The goal is to break the skins and release the juice while keeping the pulp in contact with the must — the skins carry yeast and, in the case of plums and grapes, contribute tannins that add structure to the finished wine.

**Hard fruit** (apples, pears, quinces): these need more force. A food processor, a juicer, or a dedicated fruit crusher will break them down. For cider-style ferments, many people press the juice out entirely and ferment juice only. For country-style wines you can ferment on the pulp for a few days first, then press or strain.

**Citrus**: juice it and include some of the zest if you want the aromatic oils in the finished wine. Be sparing with the pith — it contributes bitterness.

**Flowers and herbs**: these aren't fruit but the principle is the same. Handle them gently, process them quickly after harvest, and don't wash them unless you absolutely have to.

## Fermentation vessel and headspace

Once your fruit is processed, it goes into your primary fermentation vessel — typically a wide-mouth container like a crock, a food-grade bucket, or a large jar. You want something roomy enough that the must can expand and bubble without overflowing, with enough headspace for active fermentation (generally at least 20% of the total volume).

Cover the vessel loosely with a cloth or paper towel held with a rubber band. You want air to be able to escape as fermentation produces CO2, but you don't want fruit flies or dust getting in. A full airlock isn't necessary at this early stage and can actually trap CO2 in a way that stresses early-stage wild yeast — loose coverage is better for the first few days.

## The first 24–72 hours

Once the fruit is processed and in the vessel, you'll start seeing activity relatively quickly if conditions are right. At room temperature with good fruit, you may see small bubbles and smell a faint yeasty, slightly fruity smell within 24 hours. This is the wild yeast waking up and beginning to eat the available sugar.

During this early period, stir or punch down the must once or twice a day. This does several things: it redistributes the yeast, keeps the fruit cap (the pulp floating on top) from drying out and growing surface mold, and helps release CO2. It also gives you a chance to observe what's happening and smell the must at each stage, which is genuinely useful information.

If you don't see activity after 72 hours in a warm environment, it's worth investigating why — though with wild fermentation, patience is often the right move before assuming something is wrong.

## A note on temperature

Wild yeast prefers moderate temperatures — somewhere in the range of 65–75°F is usually ideal. Very cold environments slow fermentation dramatically or can prevent it from starting at all. Very hot environments (above 85°F or so) can stress or kill yeast and encourage competing organisms like acetobacter, which produces acetic acid (vinegar).

If you're in a warm climate or working in summer, find a cooler spot in your home for the fermentation vessel. If it's winter and your space is cold, a slightly warmer spot — near a water heater, or wrapped in a towel — can help get things moving.
