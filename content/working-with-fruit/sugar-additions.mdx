# Sugar additions

When you ferment fruit, yeast converts the available sugar into alcohol. The problem is that most fruit — plums, peaches, berries, even many apple varieties — doesn't naturally contain enough fermentable sugar to produce wine with a stable alcohol level. You can end up with something that tastes lovely but sits at 6 or 7% ABV, which isn't high enough to inhibit spoilage organisms reliably. The wine can turn vinegary, or just go flat and strange over a few weeks.

Adding sugar to the must before fermentation — a practice called **chaptalization** in formal winemaking circles — closes that gap. You're not making the wine artificially sweet; you're giving the yeast enough raw material to do their job fully.

## How much alcohol do you actually need?

For a fruit wine you want to store at room temperature for any length of time, aim for a finished ABV of at least 11%, and ideally 12–13%. In that range, alcohol itself acts as a preservative, keeping most spoilage organisms from gaining a foothold. Below that threshold you're relying more heavily on acidity, cold storage, or drinking the wine quickly to keep it in good shape.

Cider is a partial exception — apple juice naturally ferments to 6–8% ABV, and traditional farmhouse ciders are often consumed relatively fresh from cool cellars. But for most fruit wines, 11%+ is the target.

## The relationship between sugar and alcohol

Yeast converts sugar to alcohol at a predictable ratio. The simplified version of the math is:

```
(OG − FG) × 131.25 = ABV%
```

Working backwards from that, if you want 12% ABV and expect your wine to finish around 1.000 (dry), you need:

```
Target OG = (12 ÷ 131.25) + 1.000 = 1.091
```

So your must needs to start at approximately 1.091 specific gravity to have enough sugar for 12% ABV.

## Reading your juice before adding anything

Before you add any sugar, take a hydrometer reading of your juice or must. This tells you what the fruit is naturally contributing, and from there you can calculate exactly how much sugar is needed to reach your target.

A typical plum must, for example, might read around 1.045–1.055. Apple juice usually comes in around 1.048–1.060. Berries vary widely. Whatever your reading is, that's your starting point.

## Calculating your sugar addition

A useful rule of thumb: **1 lb of granulated sugar added to 1 gallon of must raises the specific gravity by approximately 0.046** (46 gravity points).

The formula:

```
Sugar needed (lbs) = (target OG − current OG) ÷ 0.046 × gallons of must
```

**Example:** You have 3 gallons of plum must reading 1.050. You want to reach 1.091.

```
Gravity gap: 1.091 − 1.050 = 0.041
Per gallon: 0.041 ÷ 0.046 = 0.89 lbs
Total: 0.89 × 3 = 2.7 lbs of sugar
```

Round to the nearest convenient amount, dissolve the sugar in a small amount of warm water first (this makes it much easier to incorporate), let it cool to room temperature, and stir it into the must. Then take another hydrometer reading to confirm you've hit your target before sealing up the vessel.

## Adding sugar in stages

You don't have to add all your sugar at the beginning. Adding it in stages — sometimes called a **step feed** or **staggered addition** — has real advantages, especially with wild ferments.

A very high sugar concentration at the start of fermentation stresses yeast osmotically: the sugar-dense liquid draws water out of the yeast cells, which can inhibit or slow fermentation. Adding sugar in smaller amounts over the course of the first week or two keeps the environment more hospitable and gives wild yeast — which tends to be less robust than commercial strains — more time to establish before encountering high sugar levels.

A reasonable approach: add enough sugar at the start to reach around 1.070–1.080, then check the gravity after a few days of active fermentation and add another increment to push toward your final target. You can do this two or three times across the first week without any problem.

## What kind of sugar to use

Plain white granulated cane sugar is the standard choice, and there's nothing wrong with it. It's neutral in flavor, fully fermentable, and easy to find. A few alternatives worth knowing:

**Brown sugar and raw sugar** contribute trace molasses flavor, which can work well with stone fruit or anything that benefits from a slightly earthy depth. The molasses content is partially fermentable but adds subtle flavor compounds that survive into the finished wine.

**Honey** is used in mead and can be used in fruit wine too, where it adds floral complexity. It's more expensive and has a slightly different fermentation character — honey fermentations tend to start more slowly and need a little extra attention.

**Corn sugar (dextrose)** is sometimes recommended because it's a monosaccharide (yeast can use it directly without breaking it down first), but in practice the difference in a fruit wine is negligible.

Avoid artificial sweeteners — they're not fermentable and will make your wine cloyingly sweet in an artificial way. If you want residual sweetness in the finished wine, there are better ways to achieve it.

## A note on sorbitol in stone fruit

One thing that surprises people when they first work with plums: plums taste very sweet, but they contain significant amounts of **sorbitol**, a sugar alcohol that yeast cannot ferment. A plum that tastes as sweet as a grape may actually contribute far less fermentable sugar than the taste suggests.

This is why plum wine almost always requires meaningful sugar additions — you're not just supplementing, you're compensating for the fact that a significant portion of the fruit's sweetness is locked up in a form yeast can't touch. Don't be surprised if your plum must reads lower than you expected.
