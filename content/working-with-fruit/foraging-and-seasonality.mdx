# Foraging and seasonality

There's something particular about making wine from fruit you found rather than fruit you bought. The plum tree in a neighbor's backyard that drops more fruit than any family could eat. Blackberries growing wild along a trail you walk regularly. Elderflowers in bloom for three weeks in late spring before they're gone for another year. These aren't just ingredients — they're the place you live, made fermentable.

Wild fermentation and foraged ingredients belong together in more than a philosophical sense. Fruit that has grown undisturbed in your local environment carries the wild yeast community of that specific place. When you ferment it, you're capturing something genuinely local — the microorganisms of your neighborhood, in this season, from this particular tree or hedgerow. No commercial product replicates that.

## Why foraged fruit is exceptional for wild fermentation

Foraged and garden fruit tends to carry better wild yeast populations than commercially grown fruit for several reasons. It hasn't been treated with fungicides. It ripens on the plant rather than in a warehouse. It's been exposed to the local microbial environment for its entire development. And it's usually picked at or very near full ripeness, when wild yeast populations on the surface are at their peak.

The bloom on a ripe foraged plum — that faint dusty coating — is largely yeast. Touch it, and you've inoculated your fingertips with wild Saccharomyces and dozens of other species. Handle your fruit carefully to preserve it.

## What to look for

**Abundance and accessibility.** Foraging works best with fruit that's genuinely abundant — where taking a meaningful quantity doesn't deplete the source. A tree laden with more fruit than will ever be eaten, brambles that produce far more than local wildlife can consume, a park with multiple elderflower-covered shrubs — these are the right targets.

**Condition.** Foraged fruit should be at or near full ripeness, free of visible mold, and not obviously stressed. Fruit that's dropped to the ground and sat there isn't ideal — it's more likely to have begun fermenting in ways you didn't choose, or to have been colonized by organisms you don't want. Fruit still on the plant or freshly fallen is better.

**Location.** Avoid foraging from roadsides with heavy traffic, near agricultural fields that may have been sprayed, or in areas where runoff from nearby land use might be a concern. Parks, residential neighborhoods, established trails away from roads, and friends' or neighbors' gardens are generally good. When in doubt about a location, it's worth asking or skipping.

**Identification.** For fruit that could be confused with anything toxic — certain berries especially — be certain of what you have before you pick it. Elderberries, for example, have a look-alike in pokeweed that's toxic. Foraging guides specific to your region, combined with a few conversations with experienced local foragers, will build your confidence faster than any general guide.

## Working with what the season offers

Seasonal foraging shapes your fermentation calendar in a way that buying fruit at a store doesn't. What's available determines what you can make, which means you end up fermenting things you might never have chosen deliberately — and discovering that they're extraordinary.

The approach that works best is to pay attention throughout the year, note what appears and when, and be ready to act quickly when something peaks. Wild fruit doesn't wait. A plum tree that's dropping ripe fruit today may be finished in a week. Elderflowers are notoriously brief — two to three weeks of bloom, then gone. Blackberries have a longer window but still reward daily attention at peak season.

Over a year or two of paying attention, you'll develop a mental map of what's available in your area and when, and you'll find yourself planning batches in advance rather than reacting opportunistically. That annual rhythm — something new becoming available every few weeks through the growing season — is one of the genuine pleasures of fermentation as a practice.

## The ethics of foraging

Foraging responsibly means leaving more than you take and leaving the source in good condition.

**Take only what you'll use.** It's easy to get excited and pick more than you can process before it deteriorates. Know how much fruit you need for your batch, pick a little more as a buffer, and stop.

**Leave fruit for wildlife.** Birds, insects, and mammals depend on wild fruit as a food source. A good general rule is to take no more than a third of what's available at any location, and ideally less.

**Ask before picking on private property.** Many people with fruit trees are delighted to have someone take excess fruit they'd otherwise watch rot. A knock on the door and a friendly question costs nothing and often opens up an ongoing relationship — and maybe a bottle of wine in return at the end of the season is a very natural way to say thank you.

**Don't damage the plant.** Twist or cut fruit cleanly. Don't break branches to reach higher fruit. Elderflowers should be cut with scissors rather than torn, and you should leave plenty of flower heads to develop into berries for birds later in the season.

## A note on seasonality wherever you are

The specific seasonal rhythms of your area will be different from anywhere else — different climate, different native and introduced plants, different timing. Part of the pleasure of paying attention is discovering what your particular place offers.

What's consistent everywhere: wild fermentation works best when you're using what's genuinely in season locally, at its peak, from as close to home as possible. That's not a rule — it's just an observation that the best wild-fermented wines I've made have come from fruit I didn't have to travel far to find, picked when it was ready, used quickly. The terroir argument for local foraging is real: the wine you make from your neighbor's plum tree will taste like those plums, from that tree, in that season. You can't buy that.
