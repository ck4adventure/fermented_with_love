# Monitoring your ferment

One of the things nobody tells you when you start fermenting is that your senses are the most important monitoring tools you have. A hydrometer gives you numbers; your nose and palate give you context. The goal over time is to develop enough intuition that you can walk up to a fermentation vessel, take a smell, taste a small sample, look at what's happening on the surface, and have a clear sense of whether everything is going well or something needs attention.

That intuition takes time to build — but it builds faster if you pay attention deliberately from the start, rather than just checking in when something seems wrong.

## How often to check

During **primary fermentation** (the first one to three weeks, while the must is actively bubbling and you're stirring or punching down the cap), check in at least once daily. You're punching down anyway, so this is just a matter of also taking a moment to smell and observe. Twice daily is better — morning and evening gives you a complete picture of the daily rhythm of the ferment.

During **secondary fermentation** (the quieter phase in a sealed vessel with an airlock), check every few days. You're looking for continued slow bubbling, the absence of anything alarming, and the gradual accumulation of sediment that tells you things are progressing. A hydrometer reading every one to two weeks during this phase gives you useful data on how quickly gravity is dropping.

## What a healthy ferment looks like

### Days 1–3: waking up

A healthy wild ferment starting up looks like not much at all, then suddenly like something. In the first 24 hours you may see nothing visible. By 48–72 hours, if conditions are right, you should see small bubbles beginning to rise through the must, a faint froth starting to form at the surface, and the fruit solids beginning to push upward.

The color of the must will begin to shift — plum must starts deep red-purple and will stay that way; apple must starts pale golden; berry musts go vivid and jewel-toned as pigments extract from the skins. This color development is a sign that things are moving.

### Days 3–10: active fermentation

This is the most dramatic stage. The cap is rising noticeably between punchdowns. Bubbles are vigorous and constant. When you punch down and put your face over the vessel, you'll feel a gentle warmth from the CO2 releasing — the fermentation is generating a small amount of heat. The surface after punching down will quickly fill with fine foam.

During active fermentation, the must is surprisingly beautiful to look at — vivid, turbulent, alive. This is the stage where it most obviously resembles something happening rather than something sitting still.

### Weeks 2–4: slowing down

The vigorous bubbling subsides. The cap rises less dramatically between punchdowns and eventually stops rising at all, which is your signal to rack the wine off the solids and into a sealed secondary vessel. In that vessel, you'll see occasional slow bubbles through the airlock — every few minutes rather than constantly. The wine begins to lose its vivid opacity and you can start to see through it slightly.

### Secondary: clarifying

Over the following weeks and months, the wine gradually clears from the top down. The bottom of the vessel stays murky with lees while the upper portion clears first. You'll see a distinct line between the clear wine above and the haze below. Each racking moves that line lower and leaves more sediment behind. Eventually — and this can take several months for a wild ferment — the wine is clear enough to see through.

## What a healthy ferment smells like

Smell is where fermentation becomes most visceral, and developing a vocabulary for it is worth the effort. The progression of smells through a wild fermentation is one of the most interesting things about the process.

### Early fermentation: yeast and fruit

At the very start — the first day or two of activity — a wild ferment smells primarily of the fruit itself, maybe slightly bruised or crushed, with a faint background note of something yeasty and slightly funky beginning to develop underneath. It's not quite bread dough and not quite beer — it's something more raw and alive than either.

As fermentation gets going in earnest, the yeast smell becomes more prominent. With wild fermentations specifically, this early smell can be quite complex — sometimes floral, sometimes slightly animal, occasionally with a faint sulfurous background that usually resolves as fermentation progresses. This complexity is the wild yeast community doing what it does, and it smells different every time depending on what fruit you're using and who showed up.

### Active fermentation: the bloom

During peak activity, a healthy ferment has what I can only describe as a bloom — a full, rounded, almost fizzy aromatic quality that's somewhere between ripe fruit and rising bread and something entirely its own. It fills the room. If you lean over the vessel, you may feel a slight prickling in your nose from the CO2.

Stone fruit fermentations at this stage smell deeply of the fruit — plum wine during peak fermentation smells intensely of plum, more concentrated and vivid than the fresh fruit did. Berry fermentations smell jammy and slightly spiced. Apple fermentations develop a complex cidery quality that bears little resemblance to apple juice. These peak aromas are one of the reasons fermentation is so compelling to work with — the smell of an active ferment is extraordinary.

### Slowing fermentation: alcohol emerging

As fermentation slows, the fruit aromatics soften and the alcohol becomes more present in the smell. The must smells drier, slightly warmer, less immediately fruity. This is the wine beginning to find its character. The wild yeast community has been producing esters and other aromatic compounds throughout, and you can begin to smell their contribution — floral notes, a slight earthiness, complexity that isn't coming from the fruit alone.

### Secondary and aging: integration

In secondary fermentation and aging, the smell becomes quieter and more integrated. You're no longer smelling the active process of transformation so much as the accumulating result of it. Young wine in secondary often smells slightly closed — the aromas are there but muted, as if still finding their form. Over months, the wine opens. What was sharp softens. What was simple becomes layered.

### Off smells to know

**Rotten egg or struck match:** Hydrogen sulfide, produced by stressed yeast under nitrogen deficiency. Acts quickly — splash the must between containers to drive it off. If it recurs, add yeast nutrients.

**Sharp vinegar:** Acetobacter activity. Punch down the cap more frequently to reduce oxygen exposure. A slight vinegar edge in very young wine sometimes resolves; a wine that strongly smells of vinegar is headed in a direction that won't improve.

**Nail polish remover (ethyl acetate):** A close relative of the vinegar problem, also oxygen-related. More common in wild ferments than commercial ones. A small amount is part of the normal wild ferment character; a pronounced amount is a problem.

**Mustiness or wet cardboard:** Can indicate a contaminated vessel or cork taint. Rare with good sanitation practices, but if a smell like this develops in a sealed secondary vessel, it's worth investigating whether the airlock dried out and let air in.

**Nothing — totally inert:** A must that smells like neither fruit nor fermentation after 72 hours in a warm environment is not fermenting. Investigate before assuming everything is fine.

## What a healthy ferment tastes like

Tasting your ferment at each stage is genuinely informative — and also just interesting. The transformation from sweetened fruit juice to wine happens in stages you can follow on your palate if you pay attention.

### Must before fermentation

Sweet, bright, clearly of the fruit. The sugar is fully present and unmodified. If you've added enough sugar to hit your OG target, it may taste quite sweet — more so than you'd want in a finished wine, which is expected. The fruit character is clean and direct, without the complexity that fermentation will add.

### Days 2–5: early fermentation

The sweetness is beginning to recede. There's a slight prickling on the tongue from CO2 in suspension — a faint fizziness that isn't unpleasant. The fruit character is still vivid but has taken on a slightly fermented quality — still recognizably plum or apple or whatever you started with, but with a yeasty, slightly funky edge beginning to emerge underneath. It tastes like something in the middle of becoming.

### Days 7–14: active fermentation

Noticeably drier than early on — you can feel the sugar dropping. The alcohol is present and warming. The fruit character has shifted from fresh to something more cooked or concentrated. Wild ferments at this stage often have an interesting complex funk that commercial-yeast wines don't — earthy, slightly animal, alive in a way that's hard to describe precisely. This is the wild yeast community at its most expressive. It may not taste polished, but it's fascinating.

### End of primary: racking time

The sweetness is largely gone if you're heading toward a dry wine. The alcohol is clearly present. The fruit character has softened and integrated with the fermentation character. It tastes young and rough around the edges — not something you'd want to drink right now, but you can begin to see the shape of what it's going to be.
