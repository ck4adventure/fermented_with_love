# Racking

At some point during fermentation, your wine transitions from a murky, active, fruit-filled must into something that needs a quieter environment to finish and clarify. That transition is managed through **racking** — the process of moving the wine from one vessel to another, leaving behind the sediment that has settled at the bottom.

It sounds simple, and the mechanics of it are. But understanding *when* to rack, and *why*, makes the difference between wine that clarifies beautifully and wine that stays cloudy and develops off-flavors.

## What you're leaving behind

The sediment at the bottom of your fermentation vessel — called the **lees** — is made up of dead and dying yeast cells, fruit solids, and various compounds that have precipitated out of the wine as fermentation has progressed. Some contact with the lees is beneficial: it adds complexity and a certain richness to the wine. Extended contact with the lees is a different matter.

As yeast cells die and break down in a process called **autolysis**, they release compounds into the wine that can produce off-flavors — yeasty, meaty, or rubbery notes that don't belong in a fruit wine. The longer the wine sits on heavy lees after fermentation has slowed, the more pronounced these flavors can become.

The goal is to rack off the gross lees (the heavy initial sediment) while fermentation is still quietly finishing, and then rack again once a secondary layer of finer sediment has formed.

## When to rack for the first time

The first rack should happen when:

- Primary fermentation has visibly slowed — bubbling has reduced significantly
- The cap is no longer rising actively
- A hydrometer reading shows gravity still slowly dropping but no longer crashing
- There is a distinct, thick layer of sediment at the bottom of your vessel

For wild ferments this is typically somewhere between 1–3 weeks after you started, depending on temperature and yeast activity. You don't have to wait until fermentation is completely finished — in fact, a small amount of remaining activity is fine and actually helps the wine in its new vessel, as the CO2 produced will displace oxygen that might otherwise cause oxidation.

## The second and subsequent racks

After the first rack, fermentation will continue slowly in the new vessel (now sealed with an airlock) for another few weeks to a few months, depending on how much sugar remains. During this time, another layer of finer sediment — mostly precipitated compounds and fine yeast particles — will settle to the bottom.

Rack again when you can see at least a quarter-inch of sediment at the bottom of the vessel, or every 4–6 weeks, whichever comes first. Each racking produces clearer wine and removes material that could affect flavor during long aging.

For most fruit wines you'll rack two or three times before bottling. Wild ferments sometimes take longer to clarify than commercial-yeast wines, so don't be in a rush.

## What you need

**A siphon or auto-siphon:** The essential racking tool. A simple two-piece siphon (a rigid tube and a flexible hose with a clamp) works fine. An auto-siphon, which has a built-in pump mechanism, makes starting the siphon much easier and reduces the chance of accidentally disturbing the sediment. If you're going to buy one piece of equipment for this process, an auto-siphon is it.

**A receiving vessel:** Clean, sanitized, and appropriately sized. You want to minimize headspace (the air gap above the wine) in the receiving vessel, as this is where oxidation risk lives. If your wine doesn't fill the new vessel completely, you can top it up with a small amount of water, or with a similar wine if you have one.

**An airlock:** Once the wine is in its secondary vessel, seal it with a bung and airlock. The airlock lets CO2 escape while preventing air from getting in.

## How to rack

1. Place your current vessel on a table or counter, and the clean receiving vessel on the floor below it. You're using gravity, so the height difference matters.

2. Insert your siphon into the current vessel, taking care to position the intake end well above the sediment layer — usually about an inch above the bottom. If you disturb the sediment while racking, the wine you're transferring will carry it with you, which defeats the purpose.

3. Start the siphon (pump the auto-siphon, or use suction if using a traditional siphon) and let the wine flow into the receiving vessel.

4. Watch the level in the original vessel as it drops. As you approach the sediment, tilt the vessel slightly to drain as much clear wine as possible without pulling the sediment in. Stop when you start seeing cloudiness enter the siphon tube.

5. The small amount of liquid left in the original vessel with the sediment can be discarded, or passed through a fine mesh strainer into a separate container and allowed to settle further before being added back if you want to preserve every drop.

6. Fit the airlock and move the vessel somewhere stable and reasonably cool (60–70°F) to continue clarifying.

## Topping up and headspace

Headspace — the air gap between the wine surface and the bung — is one of the more significant sources of oxidation risk during secondary fermentation and aging. After racking, your goal is to minimize it as much as possible.

A gallon-sized carboy with half an inch of headspace is fine. Several inches of headspace over weeks or months is a problem.

If your wine doesn't fill the new vessel, top it up. Options:

- Similar wine (another batch of the same fruit, or something complementary)
- Water, if the flavor dilution is acceptable (usually fine for a small amount)
- A similarly sized collection of marbles or glass weights to displace the air gap (old-fashioned but genuinely effective)

## What racking is not

Racking is not filtering. It won't produce sparkling-clear wine on its own — it just removes gross sediment and improves conditions for natural clarification over time. If you want faster clarification, there are fining agents that can help. But for most wild fruit wines, patience and careful racking will get you where you want to go.
