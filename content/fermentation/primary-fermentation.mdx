# Primary fermentation

Primary fermentation is the main event — the stage where most of the sugar gets converted to alcohol and the must transforms from sweetened fruit juice into something that's starting to become wine. It's also the most visually active stage, which makes it the most satisfying to watch.

## The vessel

For primary fermentation you want a wide-mouth container. A food-grade plastic bucket, a large ceramic crock, a glass jar, or a wide-mouth carboy all work well. Wide mouths are practical because you need to stir and punch down the fruit cap regularly during the first few days, and also because the early stages of wild fermentation benefit from having some contact with the ambient environment — a narrow-neck vessel with an airlock is more appropriate for the secondary stage.

Size matters here: your must needs room to expand and bubble. Aim for a vessel at least 30% larger than your total volume. If you have a gallon of must, a container that holds at least a gallon and a half gives you the headspace you need.

**Cover the vessel loosely** during primary fermentation. A clean cloth or paper towel secured with a rubber band works well. This lets CO2 escape while keeping dust, insects, and airborne contaminants out. A full airlock at this stage isn't ideal for wild fermentation — CO2 needs to be able to vent freely during the most active period, and a sealed system can create pressure that stresses early-stage wild yeast.

## What happens during primary fermentation

Within the first 24–72 hours of a wild ferment, assuming good fruit and a reasonable temperature (65–75°F), you should start seeing signs of activity:

- Small bubbles rising through the must
- A faint hissing or fizzing sound when you lean close
- A yeasty, slightly sweet and fruity smell
- Fruit solids starting to rise to the top of the liquid, pushed up by CO2 bubbles

That layer of solids floating on the surface is called the **cap**. It's completely normal and actually desirable during primary fermentation — the skin contact adds tannins, color, and flavor compounds to the wine. But it needs attention: left alone, the cap dries out and can develop surface mold, which you don't want.

## Punching down the cap

Once or twice a day during active fermentation, use a clean spoon or your hand to push the cap down into the liquid. This is called **punching down**, and it does several things at once:

- Keeps the cap moist and prevents surface mold
- Redistributes the yeast throughout the must
- Releases CO2 that's trapped in the cap
- Gives you a direct look and smell at what's happening

That daily interaction with the ferment is worth more than you might expect. After a few days you develop a sense of what active fermentation smells like, how it changes over time, and when something might be off. You can't get that from checking through a glass vessel without opening it.

## How long does primary fermentation last?

With wild fermentation, this varies more than it would with a commercial yeast. A rough guideline:

- Most active period (most visible bubbling): 3–7 days
- Fermentation quiets but continues: another 1–2 weeks
- Ready to move to secondary: when bubbling has slowed significantly, the cap is no longer rising vigorously, and a hydrometer reading shows gravity dropping consistently

For wild ferments specifically, don't rush this transition. Commercial yeast fermentations can move faster and more predictably. Wild fermentations sometimes pause, restart, and take longer paths to the finish. As long as you're not smelling something genuinely wrong — sharp vinegar, acetone (nail polish remover), or rotten egg — let it work at its own pace.

## Signs that something needs attention

Most fermentations proceed without drama, but here are things worth paying attention to:

**No activity after 72–96 hours at room temperature:** Could be a cold environment slowing things down, or very low natural yeast on the fruit. Try moving to a warmer spot (not hot — just slightly warmer, 70°F+) and stirring more vigorously to introduce a little oxygen and redistribute anything that's settled.

**Gray or white surface mold on the cap:** Usually means the cap dried out between punchdowns. Remove the mold carefully from the surface — don't stir it in — and punch down more frequently going forward. The wine underneath is almost certainly fine.

**Pink-tinged or slimy surface with a sharp vinegar smell:** This is acetobacter, the organism that turns alcohol to acetic acid (vinegar). It needs oxygen to thrive, so it tends to grow on exposed surfaces. Remove the affected layer, punch down, and cover the vessel more carefully.

**Rotten egg smell (hydrogen sulfide):** A stressed yeast response, often due to nutrient deficiency. In a wild ferment this can happen if the must was very low in nitrogen. Splashing the must vigorously (pouring it from one vessel to another) can help drive off the sulfur compound. Addressing this early is much easier than addressing it later.

## Tasting during primary fermentation

You can and should taste the must during this stage. It will change a lot over the first week — starting sweet and cloudy and fruity, then becoming drier, more complex, and less sweet as the yeast works through the sugar. Tasting regularly builds your intuition about what a ferment at each stage should taste and smell like. It also gives you early warning of problems, because your palate will often catch something before it becomes serious.

Don't be alarmed that it tastes like fizzy, slightly alcoholic, cloudy fruit juice at this stage. That's exactly what it is. Wine happens later.
