# Glossary

Winemaking has its own vocabulary, and some of it is genuinely useful — precise terms that let you communicate clearly with other makers and at homebrew shops. Others are just jargon that sounds intimidating until you know what it means.

This is meant to be a reference you can return to whenever you hit an unfamiliar term, not something to read front to back. Terms are alphabetical.

---

**ABV (alcohol by volume)**
The percentage of a liquid that is ethanol (alcohol). Expressed as a percentage — a wine at 12% ABV is 12% alcohol by volume. Calculated from the difference between original and final gravity: (OG − FG) × 131.25 = ABV%.

---

**Acetobacter**
A genus of bacteria that converts alcohol into acetic acid (vinegar). Acetobacter needs oxygen to thrive, which is why it tends to establish at the surface of a must — on a drying fruit cap, or in excessive headspace. A small amount of volatile acidity from acetobacter activity is normal and can contribute complexity; a lot of it makes vinegar.

---

**Acetic acid**
The acid that gives vinegar its characteristic sharp smell and taste. Produced by acetobacter acting on alcohol in the presence of oxygen. A wine with noticeable acetic acid is said to have high **volatile acidity**. Small amounts are part of the normal flavor profile of many wines; high amounts indicate a spoilage problem.

---

**Acid blend**
A commercial mixture of tartaric, malic, and citric acids used to increase the acidity of a must or finished wine. Added when a must tastes flat or when pH testing shows the must is too low in acidity for a stable fermentation.

---

**Airlock**
A small plastic device fitted to the neck of a fermentation vessel that allows CO2 to escape while preventing air from entering. Filled partway with water (or a sanitizer solution), which acts as a one-way valve. Used during secondary fermentation and aging to protect the wine from oxidation and acetobacter.

---

**Autolysis**
The process by which dead yeast cells break down and release their contents into the surrounding wine. In small amounts and with certain yeast strains, autolysis can add desirable complexity (it's deliberately encouraged in Champagne production). In fruit wines sitting too long on heavy lees, autolysis produces off-flavors — meaty, rubbery, or yeasty notes. This is the main reason to rack the wine off gross lees in a timely way.

---

**Back-sweetening**
Adding sugar (or another sweetener) to a finished wine after fermentation is complete, to increase residual sweetness before bottling. Because the wine still contains live yeast, back-sweetened wine must either be stabilized with potassium sorbate (which prevents yeast from reproducing and restarting fermentation) or consumed relatively quickly. Otherwise the added sugar will ferment out, and a sealed bottle may overcarbonate.

---

**Bentonite**
A type of clay used as a fining agent to clarify wine. Mixed with water to form a slurry and stirred into the wine, where it binds to protein particles and causes them to clump and settle. Food-grade and vegan. One of the most commonly used fining agents in white and fruit winemaking.

---

**Brix**
A scale for measuring sugar content in a liquid, expressed as grams of sugar per 100 grams of solution. Used in commercial winemaking and on some hydrometers. One degree Brix is roughly equivalent to a specific gravity of 1.004. Either scale works for home use — specific gravity tends to be easier for beginners to work with.

---

**Bung**
A rubber or silicone stopper that fits into the neck of a carboy or fermentation vessel and holds the airlock in place. Comes in different sizes to fit different vessel necks — buy the right size for your carboy.

---

**Cap**
The layer of fruit solids (skins, pulp, seeds) that floats to the surface of a must during primary fermentation, pushed up by CO2 bubbles. The cap carries wild yeast and contributes color, tannins, and flavor compounds. During primary fermentation, the cap should be punched down at least once daily to prevent it from drying out and developing surface mold, and to redistribute the yeast.

---

**Carboy**
A large narrow-necked glass or plastic vessel used for secondary fermentation and aging. The narrow neck minimizes headspace and accepts a bung and airlock. Glass carboys (typically one-gallon or five-gallon) are preferred for long-term aging because glass is non-reactive and doesn't absorb flavors. One-gallon glass jugs — the kind apple juice or water is sold in — are a very practical and inexpensive option for small batches.

---

**Chaptalization**
The addition of sugar to a must before fermentation to increase the potential alcohol level. Named after Jean-Antoine Chaptal, a French chemist who popularized the technique in the early 19th century. Standard practice in fruit winemaking because most fruit doesn't naturally contain enough fermentable sugar to produce a stable, shelf-worthy wine.

---

**Cold crash**
The practice of moving a fermentation vessel to a near-freezing environment (32–40°F) for several days to a week to encourage clarification. Cold temperatures cause suspended particles — yeast, proteins, tartrate crystals — to clump and settle more rapidly than they would at cellar temperature. After cold crashing, rack the wine off the settled sediment while keeping the vessel cold if possible.

---

**DAP (diammonium phosphate)**
A yeast nutrient and the most direct source of yeast-assimilable nitrogen (YAN). Often added to fruit musts and meads that are low in natural nitrogen, to prevent nutrient-stressed yeast from producing hydrogen sulfide and potentially stalling before fermentation completes. Usually used in combination with other nutrient products rather than alone.

---

**Ester**
A class of aromatic compounds produced by yeast during fermentation. Esters contribute a wide range of fruity and floral aromas — ethyl acetate (pear drop), isoamyl acetate (banana), ethyl hexanoate (apple) — and are a significant part of what makes wild fermentations smell and taste complex. Different yeast strains and species produce different ester profiles, which is one reason wild fermentations often have more aromatic interest than commercial-yeast wines.

---

**FG (final gravity)**
The specific gravity reading of a wine when fermentation is complete, with all or most fermentable sugar consumed. For a fully dry wine this is typically around 1.000 or slightly below. For a semi-sweet wine it will be higher — around 1.005–1.015 depending on how much residual sugar remains. Fermentation is confirmed complete when the same FG reading is obtained two days in a row.

---

**Fining**
The process of adding a substance (a fining agent) to wine to clarify it by binding to suspended particles and causing them to settle. Common fining agents include bentonite (for proteins), gelatin (for tannins and particulates), isinglass (for fine particles), and Sparkolloid. Not always necessary — patient racking and cold crashing often achieve adequate clarity on their own.

---

**Gross lees**
The heavy initial sediment that settles during and immediately after primary fermentation. Composed of fruit pulp, dead yeast, seeds, and other solids. The wine should be racked off the gross lees relatively promptly after primary fermentation quiets — extended contact with gross lees encourages autolysis and off-flavor development.

---

**Headspace**
The air gap between the surface of the wine and the top of the vessel or bung. Minimizing headspace is important during secondary fermentation and aging because oxygen in the headspace promotes oxidation. After racking, top up the vessel to reduce headspace, using water, a similar wine, or glass weights if needed.

---

**Hydrogen sulfide (H₂S)**
A gas produced by nutrient-stressed yeast, specifically associated with nitrogen deficiency. Smells strongly of rotten eggs or struck matches. Volatile and can be driven off by splashing the wine (pouring it between vessels with agitation). Should be addressed promptly because H₂S can react with other wine compounds and become more difficult to remove over time. A sign to add yeast nutrients if it recurs.

---

**Lees**
The sediment that collects at the bottom of a fermentation vessel — dead yeast cells, fruit particles, and precipitated compounds. **Gross lees** are the heavy early sediment; **fine lees** are the lighter, finer sediment that settles during secondary fermentation. Wines are racked off the lees to prevent autolysis and clarify the wine.

---

**Malic acid**
One of the primary acids in fruit, particularly pronounced in apples and underripe grapes. Tastes sharper and more "green apple" than tartaric acid. Can be converted to the softer lactic acid by malolactic bacteria during a secondary **malolactic fermentation** (MLF). Some winemakers encourage MLF deliberately to soften high-acid wines; others prevent it to preserve acidity.

---

**Malolactic fermentation (MLF)**
A secondary fermentation in which bacteria (primarily Oenococcus oeni) convert malic acid into the softer lactic acid, reducing the wine's total acidity and changing its flavor profile. Not technically a "fermentation" in the sugar-to-alcohol sense, but the term is standard. Common in red winemaking; sometimes desirable in high-acid fruit wines. In wild fermentation, MLF may occur naturally if the relevant bacteria are present — another reason wild wines can taste different from their commercial-yeast counterparts.

---

**Meniscus**
The curved surface of a liquid where it meets a container wall or instrument. When taking a hydrometer reading, the liquid curves slightly upward where it contacts the hydrometer stem. Read the gravity at the bottom of this curve (the flat center of the liquid surface), not where it climbs up the glass.

---

**Must**
The mixture of fruit juice, pulp, skins, and sugar that ferments into wine. Before fermentation begins, the liquid is called must; once fermentation is underway it's sometimes called fermenting must or simply wine-in-progress. The term comes from the Latin *mustum*, meaning new wine.

---

**OG (original gravity)**
The specific gravity reading of a must before fermentation begins. Used to calculate potential ABV when compared to the final gravity after fermentation. The OG reflects the total dissolved sugar content — higher OG means more sugar, more potential alcohol.

---

**Pectic enzyme (pectinase)**
An enzyme that breaks down pectin, the natural gelling compound in fruit. Added to must to prevent or resolve pectin haze — a stable, fine cloudiness common in apple, stone fruit, and berry wines that doesn't respond to racking or time. Most effective when added before fermentation starts, but can also help clarify already-finished wines with pectin haze, more slowly.

---

**pH**
A measure of acidity on a scale from 0 (most acidic) to 14 (most alkaline), with 7 being neutral. Most fruit wines ferment best in the 3.0–3.5 range. Below 3.0 the must may be too acidic for comfortable drinking; above 3.6 spoilage risk increases and wines can taste flat. pH is related to but different from the *taste* of acidity — a wine can taste tart with a relatively high pH if it contains certain aromatic acid compounds.

---

**Potassium sorbate**
A preservative added to finished wine before back-sweetening to prevent yeast from reproducing and refermenting the added sugar. Does not kill existing yeast, but prevents them from multiplying. Should be used in combination with a sulfite addition for reliable stabilization. Not appropriate for wines intended to undergo malolactic fermentation, as it can produce a geranium off-flavor if MLF bacteria are present.

---

**Primary fermentation**
The first and most active stage of fermentation, during which most of the sugar is converted to alcohol. Characterized by vigorous bubbling, foam, and a rising fruit cap (if fermenting on solids). Typically takes one to three weeks. Conducted in an open or loosely covered vessel to allow CO2 to escape freely.

---

**Punching down**
The practice of pushing the fruit cap back down into the liquid during primary fermentation, usually once or twice daily. Prevents the cap from drying out and developing surface mold, redistributes yeast throughout the must, and releases trapped CO2. Done by hand or with a clean spoon or paddle.

---

**Racking**
The process of moving wine from one vessel to another using a siphon, leaving behind the sediment (lees) that has settled at the bottom. Done once after primary fermentation to remove gross lees, and again every four to six weeks during secondary fermentation as new sediment accumulates. Each racking clarifies the wine and removes material that could cause off-flavors during aging.

---

**Residual sugar (RS)**
The sugar remaining in a wine after fermentation has completed. A fully dry wine has very low RS (below about 4 g/L, below the threshold of human perception for most people). Semi-sweet wines have noticeable RS; sweet wines have significant RS. In wild fermentation, residual sugar is influenced by how tolerant the wild yeast strains are of alcohol — many wild strains stop before fermenting all available sugar, leaving some natural sweetness.

---

**Secondary fermentation**
The slower, quieter stage of fermentation that follows primary fermentation. The wine has been moved to a sealed vessel with an airlock; bubbling continues slowly as remaining sugar is consumed. Also sometimes refers specifically to bottle conditioning (the secondary fermentation inside a sealed bottle that produces carbonation in sparkling wines).

---

**Sorbitol**
A sugar alcohol found in significant quantities in stone fruit, especially plums. Sorbitol tastes sweet but is not fermentable by wine yeast — yeast cannot convert it to alcohol. This is why plums often taste sweeter than their actual fermentable sugar content would suggest, and why plum wine almost always requires meaningful sugar additions.

---

**Specific gravity (SG)**
A measure of a liquid's density relative to pure water (which has an SG of 1.000). Used in winemaking to track sugar content: sugar dissolved in liquid makes it denser, raising the SG. As yeast consumes sugar and produces alcohol (which is less dense than water), the SG drops. Measured with a hydrometer.

---

**Tartaric acid**
The dominant acid in grapes, and the sharpest and most stable of the common wine acids. Less prevalent in most fruit wines than in grape wine, which is partly why fruit wines often taste differently balanced. Tartaric acid is the main component of commercial acid blend and can be added alone to increase acidity without the softening effect of malic acid or the citrus flavor association of citric acid.

---

**Trial jar**
A tall, narrow tube (usually plastic or glass) used to take hydrometer readings. The hydrometer must be able to float freely without touching the sides or bottom, which is why a regular drinking glass often works poorly. Inexpensive at any homebrew shop; a dedicated trial jar makes readings easier and more accurate.

---

**Volatile acidity (VA)**
The measure of acetic acid (and other volatile acids) in a wine. A small amount of VA is normal and can contribute complexity. High VA smells and tastes sharply of vinegar and indicates acetobacter activity. Cannot be reversed once significant acidity has developed.

---

**Wild yeast**
Yeast naturally occurring on fruit skins, in soil, and in the ambient environment — as opposed to commercial yeast strains cultivated and packaged for winemaking. Wild yeast populations are communities of multiple species that ferment in succession, contributing complex flavor compounds that single-strain commercial fermentations typically don't. Less predictable than commercial yeast, and generally less alcohol-tolerant, but capable of producing wines with genuine character and a sense of place.

---

**Wine thief**
A long tube, usually glass or plastic, used to draw a sample from a fermentation vessel for tasting or hydrometer measurement. A turkey baster performs the same function and is a common substitute. The advantage of a dedicated wine thief is length — it can reach to the bottom of a tall carboy without disturbing the sediment.

---

**YAN (yeast-assimilable nitrogen)**
The nitrogen content of a must in forms that yeast can actually use — primarily ammonium ions and free amino acids. Low YAN is a common cause of sluggish fermentation, hydrogen sulfide production, and stuck ferments in fruit wines and meads, which tend to be naturally low in nitrogen compared to grape must. Addressed by adding yeast nutrients (DAP, Fermaid-O, Fermaid-K).
