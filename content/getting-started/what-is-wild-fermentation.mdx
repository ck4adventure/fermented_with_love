# What is wild fermentation?

If you've ever left fruit out a little too long and noticed it starting to smell faintly boozy, you've already witnessed wild fermentation in action. That's not the fruit going bad — that's yeast, doing exactly what yeast does.

Yeast are single-celled fungi that live essentially everywhere: on fruit skins, on flower petals, in the soil, floating in the air around you. They eat sugar and produce alcohol and carbon dioxide as byproducts. This is fermentation, and it's been happening on its own, without any human intervention, for as long as fruit has existed.

Wild fermentation is simply the practice of inviting that process in deliberately, rather than introducing a commercial yeast strain from a packet.

## What makes it "wild"

When most people make wine at home, they start by killing off whatever microorganisms are already present (usually with a campden tablet, which releases sulfur dioxide), and then add a specific commercial yeast strain they bought at a homebrew shop. That yeast has been selected and cultured for predictable behavior — it ferments reliably, tolerates alcohol well, and produces a consistent flavor profile.

Wild fermentation skips all of that. You take your fruit, you process it carefully to preserve the natural yeast population living on the skins, and you let whatever is already there do the work. You're not adding anything. You're just creating the right conditions and getting out of the way.

The tradeoff is real and worth knowing upfront: wild ferments are less predictable. They can take longer to get started. They may stall at a lower alcohol level than a commercial yeast would reach. They can produce unexpected flavors — sometimes complex and interesting, occasionally off-putting. But when they work well, they produce something that tastes genuinely of the place and fruit they came from, in a way that packeted yeast simply doesn't.

## The community inside the ferment

One thing that surprised me when I started learning about this more deeply is that a wild ferment isn't just one organism doing one thing — it's a succession of different species working in rough sequence.

The earliest actors tend to be non-Saccharomyces yeasts (species like Kloeckera and Hanseniaspora) that are very sensitive to alcohol and die off once the ferment reaches around 3–5% ABV. They contribute early flavor compounds and get the fermentation started. As they fade, more alcohol-tolerant species — closer to Saccharomyces cerevisiae, the same genus as commercial wine yeast — take over and carry the ferment further.

You're not managing a single organism. You're tending a small ecosystem.

## Why it matters for how you work

Understanding that wild yeast lives on the fruit itself changes how you handle your ingredients from the very start. You want to:

- Avoid washing fruit with anything that would kill surface microorganisms (plain water is fine if needed, but try to use fruit that doesn't need washing)
- Work with fruit that hasn't been heavily sprayed with fungicides, which would deplete the surface yeast population
- Process the fruit gently and quickly, keeping it out of conditions that would encourage the wrong organisms to proliferate before fermentation begins

If you skip commercial yeast because you want a wilder, more terroir-driven result, but then scrub your fruit with soap or buy heavily treated supermarket produce, you've undermined the whole thing before you started.

## What you're signing up for

Wild fermentation is slower, less predictable, and more hands-on than using commercial yeast. You'll check on your ferments more often. You'll taste them more. You'll pay closer attention to what's happening and make decisions based on what you observe, not just what the instructions say.

That's not a downside. That's actually the point. The ferment teaches you things a packet of yeast never would.
