# A field guide to yeast

Yeast is a single-celled fungus, and there are thousands of species of it — living in soil, on fruit skins, on flower petals, floating in the air around you right now. Fermentation happens because certain species of yeast eat sugar and produce alcohol and CO2 as byproducts. Everything else in winemaking is, in some sense, just creating the right conditions for that process to happen.

Understanding a little about the biology of yeast — what it needs, what stresses it, why different strains behave differently — makes you a better fermentation troubleshooter and helps you make more intentional decisions about your process.

## Wild yeast: a community, not a strain

When people talk about wild fermentation, they often talk about it as if there's one organism doing one thing. The reality is more interesting.

The surface of a ripe fruit carries an entire community of different yeast species. The early actors tend to be non-Saccharomyces yeasts — species like *Kloeckera*, *Hanseniaspora*, and *Metschnikowia* — that are sensitive to alcohol and die off once fermentation reaches around 3–5% ABV. Despite their brief tenure, they contribute significantly to the early flavor profile: esters, aldehydes, and various aromatic compounds that give wild ferments their complexity.

As alcohol rises and these early species fade, more alcohol-tolerant species take over — primarily *Saccharomyces cerevisiae* and its relatives, the same genus found in commercial wine yeast. These carry the fermentation further, often to 10–12% ABV, before they too eventually reach their limits.

What this means practically: a wild fermentation is a succession. You're not managing a single organism through a process — you're hosting a relay race, with different runners handing off at different stages. The outcome depends on who shows up, in what numbers, and in what sequence. That variability is both the challenge and the appeal.

## Why wild yeast taps out earlier

All yeast eventually stop fermenting, and alcohol is a primary reason why. Ethanol is toxic to yeast cell membranes — as concentration rises, it disrupts the membrane's ability to regulate what passes in and out of the cell, and eventually the yeast can no longer function.

Different strains have different tolerances based on their membrane composition and stress-response mechanisms. Wild yeast populations, being mixtures of many species, tend to have lower overall alcohol tolerance than commercial strains that have been specifically selected for robustness. Most wild ferments will naturally finish somewhere between 8–12% ABV, depending on what species were present and how well-nourished they were throughout the process.

Other factors that cause fermentation to stall early:
- **Nutrient deficiency:** Yeast need more than sugar — they need nitrogen (for building proteins), vitamins like thiamine, and various minerals. Fruit juice can be surprisingly low in yeast-assimilable nitrogen, causing stress and early dropout even when sugar is still available.
- **Temperature stress:** Wild yeast are more sensitive to temperature extremes than commercial strains. Too cold (below 55°F) and they slow dramatically. Too hot (above 85°F) and they can die off prematurely, sometimes leaving a sweet, low-alcohol wine with off-flavors.
- **Osmotic stress:** Very high sugar concentrations at the start of fermentation pull water out of yeast cells by osmosis, which can stress or inhibit them before they've really gotten started. This is one reason staggered sugar additions work better than front-loading everything.

## Commercial yeast strains

Commercial wine yeasts are isolated *Saccharomyces cerevisiae* strains that have been selected over decades for specific traits: consistent behavior, high alcohol tolerance, particular flavor profiles, cold tolerance, and so on. Using them trades the variability of wild fermentation for predictability and control.

This is not a worse choice — it's a different one. Commercial yeast is the right tool for a lot of situations: when you're working with fruit that doesn't carry a good wild yeast population, when you need reliable results for a specific occasion, or when you want to understand the baseline flavor of a fruit before introducing the wild variable.

Some strains worth knowing:

**EC-1118 (Champagne/Prise de Mousse):** The workhorse. Extremely tolerant of alcohol (up to 18%), a vigorous fermenter, very neutral in flavor contribution. Used for sparkling wines, high-ABV wines, and stuck fermentation rescue. If a wild ferment stalls and you want to push it further, EC-1118 can usually get it going again. The tradeoff: its neutrality means it doesn't contribute much of its own character, which can make the finished wine a little flat if the fruit isn't doing enough work.

**71B:** Popular for fruit wines and mead. It metabolizes a portion of malic acid (one of the sharper-tasting acids in fruit), which softens the acidity of the finished wine — particularly useful for high-acid fruits like plum or berry. Produces pleasant fruity esters. Lower alcohol tolerance than EC-1118 (around 14%), which is fine for most fruit wines.

**Lalvin D47:** A white wine strain that produces rich, full body with floral and apricot notes. Good for fruit wines where you want a rounder mouthfeel. Temperature-sensitive — produces off-flavors if fermented above 65°F, so it needs a cooler environment than many home setups naturally provide.

**Red Star Côte des Blancs:** A gentle, slow fermenter that produces low amounts of alcohol and residual CO2. Good for semi-sweet wines where you want to preserve fruit aromatics and stop fermentation with some residual sweetness, though stopping fermentation reliably is its own skill.

**Lalvin K1-V1116 (Montpellier):** Very aromatic, emphasizes floral and fruity notes, good alcohol tolerance (up to 18%). Useful for wines where you want the fruit character amplified. Can be a bit overpowering with delicate fruits.

## Choosing: wild, commercial, or hybrid

There's no right answer here — it depends on what you're making, where your fruit comes from, and what kind of process you want.

**Wild-only** makes the most sense when you have excellent-quality fruit from a clean source, you're interested in terroir-driven results and don't mind variability, and you're patient enough to let the ferment work at its own pace.

**Commercial-only** makes sense when reliability matters more than character, when your fruit is supermarket-sourced and unlikely to carry a good wild population, or when you're making something for a specific occasion and can't afford a stuck ferment.

**Hybrid approach:** Start with wild fermentation and let it run until it naturally stalls or slows significantly, then pitch a small amount of commercial yeast to finish. This lets the wild community establish flavor complexity in the early stages while using a robust commercial strain to push fermentation to completion. It's a pragmatic middle path that many experienced fruit winemakers use without advertising it.

## A note on nutrients

Whichever yeast you use, nutrient availability matters. Fruit juice — especially from stone fruit and berries — can be low in yeast-assimilable nitrogen (YAN). A ferment running low on nitrogen will produce stressed yeast, which often manifest as hydrogen sulfide (rotten egg smell) and may stall before fermentation is complete.

Yeast nutrients (diammonium phosphate, or blended products like Fermaid-O) can be added to correct this. For wild ferments where you're trying to minimize additions, adding a small amount of nutrient after 24–48 hours of active fermentation (when the wild yeast are established but still working) is a reasonable compromise. You're not undermining the wild character; you're just making sure the yeast have what they need to do their job.
