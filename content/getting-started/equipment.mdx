# Equipment you actually need

One of the things I appreciate most about fruit winemaking is how little you actually need to get started. The barrier to entry is low — if you have a large jar, something to cover it with, and a way to move liquid from one container to another without making a mess, you can make wine. Everything else is refinement.

That said, a few pieces of equipment make a real difference to the quality and consistency of what you produce. Here's how to think about the difference between what's necessary and what's simply nice to have.

---

## Necessary: what you actually need

### Fermentation vessel (primary)

For primary fermentation — the first active stage where you're stirring daily and the must is still full of fruit solids — you want a wide-mouth container large enough to hold your must with room to spare. Active fermentation produces CO2 and foam, and a vessel filled right to the top will overflow.

Wide-mouth glass mason jars work beautifully for small batches (one to two gallons). They're inexpensive, easy to clean, non-reactive, and let you see exactly what's happening inside. A loose-fitting lid, a cloth secured with a rubber band, or a piece of plastic wrap with a few small holes all serve as adequate covers during this stage — you want CO2 to be able to escape freely without letting fruit flies in.

Food-grade plastic buckets (typically available at homebrew shops in 2, 4, and 6 gallon sizes) work well for larger batches. Avoid non-food-grade plastic containers — they can leach compounds into your wine, especially over longer fermentation periods.

Whatever you use, make sure it's large enough that your must fills it no more than about 75% full.

### Fermentation vessel (secondary)

Once primary fermentation quiets down and you've strained off the fruit solids, the wine moves to a sealed vessel for secondary fermentation and aging. Here you want something with a narrow neck so you can minimize the air gap above the wine (headspace), and somewhere to attach an airlock.

Glass carboys are the standard choice — one-gallon glass jugs (the kind apple juice often comes in) are perfect for small batches and easy to find. Five-gallon carboys are the standard for larger volumes. The narrow neck allows the CO2 still being slowly produced to push oxygen out, and the airlock lets that gas escape without letting air back in.

### Airlock and bung

An airlock is a small plastic device that sits in the neck of your secondary fermentation vessel. It holds a small amount of water that acts as a one-way valve: CO2 bubbles out through the water, but air can't get back in. A bung (a rubber stopper) holds the airlock in place and seals the neck of the vessel.

These cost almost nothing at a homebrew shop and are not interchangeable between vessel sizes, so buy the size that fits your carboy or jug. Fill the airlock about halfway with water — some people use a small amount of sanitizer solution instead — and check it periodically to make sure it hasn't dried out.

### Hydrometer and trial jar

A hydrometer measures the sugar content of your must, which tells you where fermentation is starting from, how it's progressing, and when it's truly finished. It's not strictly required — people made wine for centuries without one — but without it you're navigating completely blind in terms of alcohol content and fermentation completion.

A basic hydrometer costs a few dollars. You'll also need a trial jar (a tall narrow tube the hydrometer can float freely in) to take readings without putting the hydrometer directly into your fermentation vessel. A tall drinking glass works in a pinch, but a proper trial jar makes the reading easier and more accurate.

### Something to move liquid

At some point you'll need to move wine from one vessel to another — when racking off the lees, and again when bottling. The basic tool for this is a siphon: a rigid tube inserted into the upper vessel connected by flexible tubing to wherever you're sending the wine.

Starting the flow is the main challenge with a traditional siphon. Some people use suction (put the output end in your mouth briefly to draw fluid into the tube, then direct it into the receiving vessel before the wine reaches your mouth). A more elegant solution is an auto-siphon — a two-piece device with a built-in pump that starts the flow with a few pumps of the handle, no mouth contact required. Either works.

### Bottles and closures

For still wines, standard 750ml wine bottles with natural or synthetic corks are the classic choice. You'll need a corker to insert the corks cleanly — hand lever corkers are inexpensive and work well for home volumes.

Swing-top bottles (also called Grolsch-style, with a ceramic stopper and rubber gasket on a wire bail) are a practical alternative that need no additional tools. They seal well, are reusable indefinitely, and work for both still and lightly sparkling wines. They're not rated for the pressure of fully sparkling wine, but for most fruit wines with moderate or no carbonation they're entirely suitable.

Crown caps (the same caps used on beer bottles) applied with a capper are another option, and are probably the most secure seal for anything you're planning to carbonate.

### Basic cleaning supplies

Unscented dish soap and hot water, thoroughly rinsed. That's it for cleaning. The key word is *thoroughly* — any soap residue left in a vessel can inhibit fermentation and affect flavor. Rinse until you can't smell soap anymore, then rinse again.

## Nice to have: what makes things easier

### Bottle filler wand

A bottle filler wand attaches to the end of your siphon hose and has a spring-loaded valve at the tip. When you press the tip against the bottom of a bottle, wine flows; when you lift it, it stops. This fills bottles from the bottom up with no splashing (which would introduce oxygen), and when you remove the wand it leaves almost exactly the right headspace. It's a small thing that makes bottling noticeably cleaner and less stressful.

### Fine mesh straining bag

When you're fermenting on fruit solids — whole or crushed plums, berries, peaches — a fine mesh straining bag lets you contain the fruit inside the bag, which then sits in the must during primary fermentation. When it's time to rack, you lift the bag out and squeeze it gently rather than trying to strain an entire vessel of liquid through a colander. This is especially useful for small-seeded fruit like raspberries where straining gets messy.

### Kitchen scale

Sugar additions are most accurate when measured by weight rather than volume. A basic kitchen scale is also useful for tracking fruit quantities across batches so you can replicate (or adjust) results.

### Wine thief

A wine thief is a long tube, usually glass or plastic, that you insert into the fermentation vessel to draw a sample for tasting or hydrometer readings. A turkey baster does exactly the same job and most people already own one. The advantage of a dedicated wine thief is length — it can reach into the bottom of a tall carboy without disturbing the sediment.

### pH strips or meter

pH affects both fermentation behavior and the finished flavor of your wine — it's related to but not the same as how acidic the wine tastes. A basic paper pH test strip is inexpensive and gives you a rough reading. A digital pH meter is more accurate and reusable, but requires calibration and maintenance. Neither is necessary for getting started, but if you find yourself curious about why one batch tasted sharper than another, pH is often part of the answer.

## What you can skip

**Fancy temperature control equipment.** A thermometer to check your ambient temperature is useful; a dedicated fermentation temperature controller is not necessary for fruit wines unless you're in an environment with very wide temperature swings.

**A floor corker.** Hand lever corkers do the job fine for home volumes. Floor corkers are faster and easier on larger batches, but at a gallon or two at a time they're not worth the cost or storage space.

**Anything that promises to speed up fermentation.** Heating belts, ultrasonic devices, various additives marketed for this purpose — none of it makes a meaningful difference to the quality of the finished wine, and some of it can actively interfere with wild fermentation.
