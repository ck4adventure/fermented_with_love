# Flower wines

Flower wines are the most seasonal things you can ferment. A bloom window might last two weeks, sometimes less. Miss it and you wait another year. That constraint — the necessity of paying attention, of being ready when the flowers are ready — is part of what makes flower wines so satisfying to make. There's an urgency to them that other ferments don't have.

The character of a flower wine is also unlike fruit wine in a way that takes some getting used to. Fruit wines announce themselves — you can usually identify the fruit within the first second of tasting. Flower wines are more oblique. The flavor is aromatic, floral, sometimes herbaceous, with a quality that's hard to name precisely but unmistakably present. At their best they taste like the flower's fragrance translated into something drinkable.

## The challenge of delicate aromatics

The central challenge of flower winemaking is that the aromatic compounds responsible for a flower's fragrance are volatile — they evaporate readily, especially with heat and agitation. Active fermentation produces a lot of both: CO2 carries aromatic compounds out of the liquid as it bubbles through, and the activity of fermentation itself drives off volatile compounds faster than they would otherwise dissipate.

This means a flower wine that smells wonderful during fermentation may smell significantly less wonderful by the time it's finished. The aromatics that define a flower tend to fade most in the first and most active days of fermentation, which is exactly when fermentation is least controllable.

Several techniques help preserve aromatics through fermentation:

**Use more flowers than you think you need.** If the aromatics are going to fade, starting with a generous amount is the first line of defense. Most flower wine approaches use what seems like an extravagant quantity — enough flowers to fill a large jar, often several cups of petals per gallon of wine.

**Add flowers in stages.** Some makers add half the flowers at the beginning of fermentation and the other half in secondary, once the most active phase is over. The second addition contributes fresh aromatic compounds that haven't been subjected to the heat and agitation of primary fermentation.

**Cold infusion after fermentation.** Adding a final small handful of fresh flowers to the finished wine for two to four days before bottling can restore aromatic brightness that fermentation stripped away. This is particularly effective with flowers that carry a lot of their character in volatile top notes — elderflower, rose, jasmine.

**Ferment cool.** Lower fermentation temperatures slow yeast activity, reduce CO2 production, and allow volatile aromatics to stay in solution longer. The tradeoff is slower fermentation and potentially more variability with wild yeast, but for delicate flower wines the aromatic preservation is usually worth it.

## Preparation: working with flowers

Flowers require careful handling before they go into the must. A few principles that apply across most varieties:

**Harvest in the morning** after any dew has dried but before the heat of the day. Flower aromatics are most concentrated in the morning. Flowers harvested in the afternoon heat, or that have been sitting in sun for hours, are less vibrant than morning-harvested ones.

**Use flowers at peak bloom.** Not quite open — slightly underripe — isn't at full aroma. Past peak and beginning to brown produces off-flavors and loses the delicate quality you're after. Full bloom, at its freshest.

**Handle gently.** Don't crush the petals before they go into the must — crushing accelerates oxidation and can change the flavor. Drop them in whole or in loosely separated clusters.

**Remove green parts where possible.** Stems, sepals, and calyxes can contribute bitterness or vegetal flavors that compete with the floral character. With elderflowers, remove as much of the green stem as you can. With roses, the white base of each petal (where it attached to the center) can add bitterness and is worth pinching off if you have the patience.

**Don't wash flowers** if you can help it — you're removing the wild yeast population on the petals and diluting the aromatic oils. Pick clean flowers from unsprayed plants.

## Elderflower

Elderflower is the most celebrated of all flower wines and the best starting point if you've never made one. The bloom is brief — two to three weeks in late spring — but the flowers are abundant on elder shrubs, which grow in hedgerows, parks, and disturbed ground throughout much of the world. The fragrance is distinctive: muscaty, slightly lychee-like, deeply floral, with a quality that's universally recognized as "elderflower" once you know it.

Elderflower wine at its best is pale gold, lightly sparkling if you carbonate it, and fragrant in a way that's almost uncanny — it smells as much as it tastes. It's best drunk young, within a year of bottling, when the aromatics are freshest.

The flowers need to be used immediately or within a day of picking — they deteriorate quickly once harvested. If you can't use them right away, freeze them loosely and use from frozen.

## Rose

Rose petal wine is one of the most ancient flower wines and one of the most variable — the character of the finished wine depends enormously on which roses you use. Highly fragrant heritage and old garden roses (damask, Rosa gallica, many climbing varieties) produce wines with genuine depth and complexity. Modern hybrid tea roses bred for appearance rather than fragrance produce very little aromatic character in the finished wine.

Use only the petals, not the whole flower. Red and deep pink petals produce wines with a lovely blush color; white and pale petals produce nearly colorless wines where the flavor has to carry the experience without visual support.

Rose wine benefits from a small amount of tannin and good acidity — without them it can taste thin and flat despite the floral character. A small amount of lemon zest added to the must alongside the petals contributes both acid and a citrus note that complements rose well.

## Lavender

Lavender wine is intensely polarizing — people who love lavender find it extraordinary; people who don't are generally not converted by it. The flavor is unmistakably lavender, which is to say it also has a slightly soapy, herbal quality that some find refreshing and others find medicinal.

Use lavender sparingly. It's one of the most potent flowers in terms of how much flavor it contributes per unit, and a wine that tastes medicinal from overuse cannot be fixed. Start with less than you think you need — half a cup of fresh flowers or a few tablespoons of dried per gallon — and adjust based on a small test batch before committing to a larger volume.

Lavender pairs beautifully with honey as the sweetener (making it technically a metheglin), and with lemon as an acid addition. The combination of lavender and lemon is one of the most successful flower wine flavor profiles, each element tempering the intensity of the other.

## Dandelion

Dandelion wine has a long history as a spring country wine — the flowers are abundant, free, and arrive reliably as some of the first blooms of the year. The flavor is subtler than elderflower or rose — mildly floral, slightly honey-like, with an herbal edge — and the finished wine is typically pale gold and sweet.

Use only the yellow petals, not the green sepals or any stem, both of which are intensely bitter. Separating petals from the green parts is time-consuming but necessary — the bitterness from the green parts can overwhelm the delicate flavor of the petals entirely.

## The wild yeast question in flower wines

Flowers carry wild yeast — in varying amounts depending on the flower, the location, and the season. Elderflowers in a good year, from shrubs that haven't been sprayed, can carry enough yeast to start a reliable wild fermentation. Rose petals and lavender are more variable.

If you're relying on the flowers themselves for wild yeast, keep the must at room temperature, cover loosely, and be patient. Activity may take three to five days to appear. If nothing is happening after a week, add a small amount of commercial yeast rather than risk losing the batch — flower wines fermented past their peak aroma are difficult to recover.

The alternative, and often the more reliable approach for flower wines, is to use commercial yeast for the fermentation and rely on the flowers for aromatics only — adding them in cold infusion to the finished wine rather than fermenting on them at all. This sacrifices the wild fermentation character but preserves the floral aromatics much more successfully.
