# Fizzy ferments

Carbonation does something to a wine that's difficult to fully account for analytically. The bubbles lift the aromatics — you smell more of the wine with every sip because CO2 carries volatile compounds upward as it releases. The carbonic acid adds a faint prickling acidity that makes the wine feel brighter and more refreshing. The texture changes entirely. A still wine and a sparkling wine made from the same base can taste like different drinks.

This is why carbonation isn't just a stylistic choice — it's a transformation. And for many fruit wines, particularly delicate aromatic ones like elderflower, lemon, or young peach, sparkling is the right format. The bubbles do what the wine needs.

## The carbonation spectrum

Not all sparkling wines have the same level of carbonation, and the difference matters both for flavor and for how you handle and store them.

**Pétillant naturel** (often shortened to pét-nat) is the most minimal — a gentle spritz rather than true effervescence. The wine is bottled before fermentation is completely finished, with just enough residual sugar for the yeast to produce a small amount of CO2 in the bottle. The result is lightly sparkling, often slightly hazy (since the secondary fermentation happens in the bottle without disgorgement), and very much alive — you can sometimes see fine bubbles rising slowly through the wine. Pét-nat is forgiving to make, produces lower pressure than fully sparkling wines, and tends to taste vivid and fresh. It's an excellent starting point for sparkling ferments.

**Bottle-conditioned sparkling** is the method used for Champagne and traditional sparkling wines. The wine ferments to dryness, is clarified, and then a small measured amount of sugar (the *dosage* or *priming sugar*) is added at bottling to restart fermentation in the sealed bottle. The CO2 produced dissolves into the wine under pressure, producing true effervescence. This method requires more precision than pét-nat and produces higher pressure in the bottle.

**Force carbonation** involves dissolving CO2 directly into the wine using pressurized equipment — the method used for most commercial sparkling wines and ciders. Not relevant for home fermentation unless you have kegging equipment, but worth knowing exists.

## How carbonation happens in the bottle

Whether you're making pét-nat or bottle-conditioned sparkling wine, the mechanism is the same: yeast consume residual or added sugar in a sealed bottle and produce CO2 that, with nowhere to go, dissolves into the wine under pressure. When you open the bottle, the pressure drops and the CO2 comes out of solution as bubbles.

The amount of carbonation produced depends on how much sugar the yeast have available. More sugar means more CO2 means more carbonation means more pressure. This is why precision matters — not enough sugar and the wine is flat; too much and the pressure builds beyond what the bottle can safely contain.

For a detailed look at how much sugar to add and how to calculate priming amounts, see the [bottling page](/fermentation/bottling) — the mechanics of bottle conditioning are covered there alongside the equipment and technique for bottling in general.

## What the bubbles do to flavor

Beyond the texture change, carbonation affects the flavor experience in several specific ways worth understanding.

**It amplifies aromatics.** CO2 carries aromatic compounds out of the wine as it releases, which is why sparkling wine smells more intensely aromatic than the same wine still. This is particularly significant for flower wines and light citrus wines, where the aromatics are the main event. Carbonating a lemon wine or an elderflower wine is not just a stylistic choice — it's often the right choice for making the most of what the wine has.

**It increases perceived acidity.** Carbonic acid is mildly acidic, and the prickling sensation of bubbles on the palate registers as a form of acidity. A wine that tastes slightly flat or under-acidified as a still wine will often taste more alive as a sparkling one. This doesn't mean carbonation compensates for genuinely low acid — a wine that tastes truly flat when still will still have fundamental balance problems when sparkling — but it raises the apparent acidity enough to make a real difference in borderline cases.

**It reduces perceived sweetness.** The increase in acidity that comes with carbonation makes a wine taste less sweet at the same actual sugar level. This is useful to know when you're planning a sparkling wine — a level of residual sweetness that seems right in a still wine may taste slightly too dry when carbonated, and vice versa.

## Bottle safety

This is the part that deserves direct attention, because getting it wrong can cause real harm.

Glass bottles have pressure limits. Standard wine bottles are not designed to hold pressure and should never be used for actively carbonating beverages. The pressure from a secondary fermentation in a standard wine bottle can shatter it, with the predictable consequences of broken glass and wine going in all directions under pressure.

For sparkling ferments, use only:

**Champagne bottles** — thick-walled, with a punt (the indentation at the base) that strengthens the structure. Designed specifically to hold sparkling wine pressure.

**Crown-capped beer bottles** — standard glass beer bottles are pressure-rated and work well for sparkling ciders, fruit wines, and meads. The crown cap provides a reliable seal.

**Swing-top (Grolsch-style) bottles** — designed to hold moderate carbonation. Good for pét-nat and gently sparkling wines. Not rated for the full pressure of a highly carbonated wine.

Check your bottles before each use — a chip or crack in a bottle that's going to hold pressure is a serious risk. Reject anything that shows damage.

Store sparkling wines upright so the sediment from bottle conditioning settles at the bottom. Keep them somewhere cool — warmer temperatures increase the rate of CO2 production and the internal pressure. And when you open one, point it away from people and release the cork or cap slowly and deliberately. This is common sense, but worth saying.

## Pét-nat as a starting point

If you've never made a sparkling wine before, pét-nat is the right place to begin. The lower pressure and more forgiving timing make it more accessible than full bottle conditioning, and the hazy, alive quality of the finished wine is genuinely charming — it tastes handmade in the best way.

The timing for pét-nat is the main skill: you're bottling while fermentation is still very slowly active, which means reading your hydrometer carefully and developing a feel for where the ferment is. Too early and the wine overcarbonates; too late and it's flat. A reading around 1.005–1.010 — still slightly sweet, fermentation slowing but not finished — is typically the right window. You'll refine this over batches.

Your lemon wine bottled into swing-tops while still faintly fizzy, or with a sprig of mint to kick off a gentle secondary, is essentially pét-nat by another name — an intuitive version of the same technique. The more deliberate version is just that experience applied with a hydrometer.
