# Melomels and metheglins

Traditional mead has a vocabulary for its variations that sounds arcane until you know what the words mean. A **melomel** is mead with fruit added. A **metheglin** is mead with herbs or spices. A **cyser** is mead made with apple juice. A **pyment** is mead made with grape juice. These distinctions matter less than the underlying principle, which is simply: mead is a vehicle for flavor, and honey pairs well with an enormous range of additions.

Both variations follow the same basic process as traditional mead, with a few additional decisions about when and how to add the extra ingredients.

## Melomels: fruit mead

The central question with fruit additions to mead is timing — whether to add the fruit before, during, or after primary fermentation.

**Adding fruit before or at the start of fermentation** means the fruit ferments along with the honey must. You get good extraction of flavor, color, and whatever wild yeast lives on the fruit, but you also ferment away much of the fresh fruit character. The finished melomel will taste of fermented fruit integrated with honey rather than fresh fruit over honey. This works beautifully for stone fruit, dark berries, and anything where you want deep, complex fruit character rather than fresh and bright.

**Adding fruit after primary fermentation** (in secondary, once the main fermentation has quieted) is called a **fruit addition** or **back-sweetening with fruit** depending on how sweet the fruit makes the mead. Adding it at this stage preserves more of the fresh fruit character because there's less active fermentation to transform it, but the residual yeast will still consume some of the fruit sugars, so you'll get a secondary fermentation and some additional alcohol. This works well for delicate aromatics — raspberries, peaches, citrus zest — where you want the fruit character to be vivid rather than cooked-tasting.

**Both approaches** — fermenting some fruit with the must and adding more in secondary — is also a legitimate strategy, giving you both depth and freshness.

### Fruit ratios

A rough starting point: 1–2 lbs of fruit per gallon of must. More fruit produces a stronger fruit character but also adds more sugar (which affects the final ABV) and more solids (which means more work at racking time). Stone fruit and berries work at the lower end of this range; citrus, which is more aromatic than volumetrically flavorful, can be used more sparingly.

Adjust based on tasting. A melomel that tastes too honey-forward can benefit from more fruit added in secondary. One that tastes overwhelmingly of fruit and loses the honey character might benefit from a lighter hand next time.

### Fruit and wild yeast

One of the most interesting aspects of melomels is the opportunity to combine wild yeast from both sources — the raw honey and the fresh fruit. If you're working with good local honey and high-quality foraged or garden fruit, you have two sources of wild yeast contributing to the ferment. Whether they produce something coherent or chaotic depends on luck and the specific organisms present, which is exactly the kind of uncertainty that makes wild fermentation interesting to work with.

## Metheglins: herb and spice mead

A metheglin is mead flavored with botanicals — any combination of herbs, spices, flowers, bark, roots, or other plant material. The tradition is ancient and global; medieval metheglins were made with everything from ginger and cloves to rosemary and bay leaf. The category is essentially unlimited.

The most important distinction with metheglins is the difference between botanicals that ferment well alongside the honey and those that are better added after fermentation as an infusion.

### Fermenting with botanicals

Hardy, resinous herbs — rosemary, thyme, lavender, juniper berries — can go into the must at the start and hold up reasonably well through fermentation. Some of their aromatic compounds are volatile and will be carried off by CO2 during active fermentation, but their deeper, more resinous qualities survive and integrate well.

Spices with stable, heat-tolerant flavor compounds — cinnamon, cloves, star anise, black pepper — also ferment well, though they should be used sparingly. A small amount of cinnamon in a mead that's fermented for months becomes much more pronounced than it seemed at the start.

### Adding botanicals after fermentation

Delicate aromatics — citrus zest, fresh mint, lemon verbena, rose petals, jasmine, green tea — are usually better added after primary fermentation has finished, as a cold infusion. The volatile aromatic compounds that make these ingredients interesting are destroyed by heat and transformed by active fermentation. Added to finished mead and left for a few days to a week, they contribute vivid, fresh character that a full fermentation would strip away.

The approach is exactly what you might do with a bottle of finished wine: add a botanical, taste daily, and remove it when the flavor is where you want it. A few sprigs of fresh lemon verbena in a finished dry mead for four or five days will add a haunting citrus-floral quality without overwhelming the honey. Fresh mint added to a finished sparkling mead will also kick off a gentle secondary fermentation from the wild yeast still present on the herb — producing both additional carbonation and a lovely herbal dimension.

### Lemon verbena metheglin

Lemon verbena is worth particular attention here. It's one of the most aromatic herbs you can grow — the fragrance of a single leaf is astonishing, somewhere between lemon, eucalyptus, and something entirely its own. Added to a honey must with a floral, light varietal honey (orange blossom, linden, or wildflower with citrus character), and finished with a cold verbena infusion after fermentation, it produces something genuinely extraordinary. Light, aromatic, complex, and completely unlike anything commercial.

If you grow lemon verbena and are curious about mead, this is the experiment to start with.

### Ratios and caution

Botanical additions are easy to overdo. Start conservatively — a few sprigs of fresh herb, a small handful of dried flowers, a single cinnamon stick — and taste your way to where you want to be. You can always add more; you can't take it back.

Certain botanicals become unpleasant in large quantities or with extended contact. Cloves and cinnamon especially — a little is warming and interesting, a lot is medicinal. Lavender is beautiful in small amounts and soapy in large ones. Juniper can become resinous and bitter. Taste frequently, remove botanicals as soon as the flavor is right, and document what you used and for how long so you can replicate or adjust in the next batch.

## Blending honey with fruit or botanical character

One of the most interesting challenges in both melomels and metheglins is maintaining the honey character rather than letting it be overwhelmed by what you've added. A melomel where you can't taste the honey at all is really just fruit wine with honey as the sugar source. The most interesting versions are those where honey and fruit or honey and herb exist in conversation — each present, neither dominating.

This is partly a function of how much of each ingredient you use, and partly a function of which honey you choose. A delicate floral honey will be more easily overwhelmed than a robust buckwheat or wildflower honey. Matching the intensity of the honey to the intensity of the additions produces the most balanced results.

*The same principles that apply to still meads and melomels apply to sparkling versions — with the added consideration of how carbonation changes the experience of the botanicals and fruit character.*
