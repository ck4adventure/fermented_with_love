# Mead

Mead is fermented honey and water. That's the whole recipe — and also a slight understatement, because what emerges from that simple combination can range from a light, delicately floral drink to something deep and complex that rewards years of aging. It's one of the oldest fermented beverages humans have made, appearing in cultures across Europe, Africa, Asia, and the Americas long before grape wine became dominant.

If you've been making fruit wine, mead will feel familiar in some ways and genuinely different in others. The process — dissolve sugar source in water, ferment, rack, age, bottle — is the same. But honey is a unique fermentation substrate that behaves differently from fruit must, and understanding those differences will save you a lot of frustration.

## What makes mead different

**Honey is nutrient-poor.** This is the central fact about mead fermentation. Fruit juice, even fruit juice low in nitrogen, contains some amino acids, vitamins, and minerals that support yeast health. Honey is essentially pure sugar with trace minerals and some antimicrobial compounds — it contains almost no yeast-assimilable nitrogen and very little of the other nutrients yeast need to thrive.

The result, if you don't compensate, is predictable: sluggish fermentation, stressed yeast producing hydrogen sulfide, fermentation stalling well before the honey is consumed, and a finished mead that tastes thin and harsh. Nutrient management in mead isn't optional the way it sometimes is in fruit wine. It's one of the central skills of meadmaking.

**Honey has antimicrobial properties.** Raw honey resists fermentation — it's too low in water activity for most organisms to survive in it. This is why honey keeps indefinitely without refrigeration. Diluted in water to fermentable concentration, those antimicrobial properties diminish significantly, but honey must can still be a hostile environment for wild yeast getting established. This is part of what makes wild mead more challenging than wild fruit wine.

**The flavor is subtle and easily overwhelmed.** Good honey — especially single-varietal honey from a local beekeeper — has a complex, specific flavor that you want to survive fermentation into the finished mead. Heavy-handed additions, over-oaking, or aggressive fining can strip that character. The goal is usually to let the honey speak.

## Choosing honey

The honey you use matters more than any other ingredient decision. Supermarket clover honey produces drinkable mead but not particularly interesting mead — clover honey is mild, neutral, and loses most of what little character it has during fermentation. Honey with genuine varietal character — wildflower, orange blossom, buckwheat, lavender, linden, tulip poplar — carries more of that character through fermentation and produces meads worth tasting carefully.

Local honey from a beekeeper you can talk to is ideal. You'll know what the bees were foraging on, how the honey was processed, and whether it's genuinely raw (unpasteurized honey retains more aromatic compounds and a more complex flavor than heated honey). Farmers markets are a good source; many beekeepers sell directly and enjoy talking about their product.

Raw, unpasteurized honey also carries wild yeast — which brings us to wild mead.

## Wild mead

Sandor Katz, whose approach to fermentation generally emphasizes working with what's naturally present rather than what you add, argues that wild mead is entirely achievable and worth attempting. He's right, with caveats.

The challenge is that wild yeast populations in honey are sparse compared to what lives on ripe fruit skins. The antimicrobial properties of honey create a difficult starting environment. And the nutrient poverty of honey means wild yeast — which tend to be less robust than commercial strains — may struggle to establish and complete fermentation without a lot of attention and support.

That said, wild mead has been made successfully for thousands of years, before commercial yeast was a concept. The key factors that tip the balance toward success:

**Raw, local honey** carries the best wild yeast populations. Pasteurized or heavily filtered honey has far fewer viable organisms on it.

**Patience at the start.** Wild mead can take longer to show visible fermentation than wild fruit wine — sometimes four to seven days before you see activity, compared to one to three for fruit. This is normal and not a sign that something is wrong.

**Nutrient support.** Adding yeast nutrients in small staggered amounts after fermentation is clearly underway helps wild yeast survive and complete fermentation in a nutrient-poor environment. This isn't undermining the wild character; it's addressing a genuine nutritional deficit.

**Warmth.** Wild mead ferments more reliably at the warmer end of the comfortable range — 70–75°F rather than 65°F.

If wild mead doesn't take off after a week in good conditions, pitching a small amount of commercial yeast is a reasonable rescue. The wild organisms already present will continue to contribute character alongside the commercial strain.

## Commercial yeast for mead

If you want reliable results — especially for your first few meads while you're still learning what good mead tastes like — commercial yeast is the sensible choice and nothing to be apologetic about.

The yeasts that work best for mead are those with good alcohol tolerance and a track record of producing clean, honey-forward results. **71B** is the most commonly recommended for traditional meads — it metabolizes some malic acid, produces pleasant fruity esters, and tends to preserve delicate honey aromatics rather than stripping them. **EC-1118** works reliably but is so neutral that it can produce meads that lack character. **Lalvin D47** produces a rich, full-bodied mead with floral notes, but is temperature-sensitive and needs to be fermented cool (below 65°F) or it produces off-flavors.

## Basic proportions

A traditional mead targets 12–14% ABV, which requires roughly 2.5–3.5 lbs of honey per gallon of finished mead. The exact amount depends on the honey's sugar content (which varies by type and moisture level) and your target ABV.

A starting OG of 1.090–1.110 will produce a mead in that range. Take a hydrometer reading after dissolving your honey in water and adjust from there — add more honey to raise the OG, more water to lower it.

For a one-gallon batch: dissolve 2.5–3 lbs of honey in enough warm (not hot — heat destroys aromatic compounds and may kill wild yeast) water to make one gallon. Take an OG reading. Pitch yeast or leave open for wild fermentation. Add nutrients in staggered doses after fermentation begins.

## Nutrient additions for mead

This is worth taking seriously. The standard modern approach to mead nutrient additions — called TOSNA (Timed Organic Staggered Nutrient Additions) — involves adding small amounts of an organic nitrogen source (Fermaid-O is most commonly used) at specific gravity intervals during active fermentation rather than all at once at the start. This mimics what yeast experience in a nutrient-rich environment and produces healthier, more complete fermentations.

You don't need to follow a precise protocol. The principle is: add a small amount of nutrients after the first signs of fermentation, again a few days later, and again a few days after that. Don't add them all at once at the beginning. Don't add them after fermentation has largely finished.

For a one-gallon batch, a quarter teaspoon of Fermaid-O per addition, three times over the first week of fermentation, is a reasonable starting point.
