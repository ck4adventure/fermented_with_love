# Cider

Cider is what happens when you ferment apple juice, and it occupies an interesting space between fruit wine and something more distinctly its own. The process is similar enough to fruit wine that everything you already know applies directly. But cider has its own culture, its own vocabulary, and — when made with the right apples and the right approach — a character that's genuinely unlike anything else.

It's also one of the most forgiving places to start with wild fermentation. Apples carry abundant natural yeast, ferment vigorously and reliably, and produce results that are good to drink at a range of alcohol levels and sweetness points. If you've been making fruit wine and want to branch out, cider is a natural next step.

## Apple varieties and what they contribute

The apple you use matters more in cider than the particular variety of most other fruits matters in wine. Different apples contribute different balances of sugar, acid, tannin, and aromatic compounds, and the best ciders are usually made from blends that balance these elements deliberately.

A useful framework divides apples into four rough categories:

**Sharp apples** are high in acid and low in tannin. They produce ciders that are bright, crisp, and refreshing but can taste thin or one-dimensional on their own. Granny Smith, Bramley, and most cooking apples fall here.

**Sweet apples** are high in sugar and low in both acid and tannin. They ferment to a pleasant, round cider but without much structure or complexity. Golden Delicious, Fuji, and most dessert apples are in this category.

**Bittersweet apples** are high in tannin and moderate to high in sugar, low in acid. These are the classic cider apples — Dabinett, Yarlington Mill, Foxwhelp — that produce the complex, structured ciders of the West Country tradition. They're harder to find outside specialist orchards, but worth seeking out if you want to make something genuinely interesting.

**Bittersharp apples** are high in both acid and tannin — the most complex category and the most unpleasant to eat fresh. Kingston Black is the most famous example, capable of producing exceptional cider on its own.

Most people making cider at home work with whatever apples are available — farmers market culinary varieties, neighbors' backyard trees, foraged windfalls. The good news is that blending whatever you find tends to work well. A mix of sharp and sweet apples produces a balanced starting point. If you can find a single bittersweet variety to add to the blend, even a small proportion adds structure that pure dessert-apple cider lacks.

## Wild cider fermentation

Apples are one of the best fruits for wild fermentation. The skin of a ripe apple carries a generous wild yeast population, and apple juice's natural acidity creates a relatively inhospitable environment for most spoilage organisms — giving the wild yeast a head start.

Traditional farmhouse ciders from England, Brittany, and the Basque Country are almost all wild-fermented, and have been for centuries. The wild yeast community of a particular orchard is considered part of the terroir of the cider, and producers guard their wild populations carefully.

For home wild cider fermentation, the principles are the same as for wild fruit wine: use good-quality, unsprayed apples, handle them gently to preserve the surface yeast, and give the fermentation time to establish without panicking if the first 48 hours are quiet.

One difference from stone fruit and berry fermentation: apple cider tends to be fermented as juice only, after pressing, rather than on the fruit solids. This means the wild yeast has to come from what was on the apple skins and transferred to the juice during pressing, rather than ongoing contact with the fruit. Press and ferment quickly — don't let the juice sit before fermentation begins.

## Still vs sparkling

This is the main stylistic decision in cider, and it changes both the process and the experience significantly.

**Still cider** is fermented to completion and bottled without carbonation. It can be dry or have residual sweetness depending on when fermentation stops or whether you back-sweeten. Still cider is simpler to make and safer to bottle (no pressure to worry about), and it ages better than sparkling — the absence of CO2 means the delicate aromatics evolve more slowly. A still, dry cider made from interesting apples can be genuinely wine-like and worth aging for a year or more.

**Sparkling cider** is carbonated, either by bottling before fermentation is quite complete (leaving residual yeast to produce CO2 in the sealed bottle) or by adding a small amount of priming sugar at bottling to a fully fermented cider. The carbonation changes the experience considerably — it lifts the aromatics, increases the apparent acidity, and makes the cider feel lighter and more refreshing. Most commercial cider is sparkling; the farmhouse tradition is more mixed.

If you're making sparkling cider, use bottles rated for pressure — champagne bottles, crown-capped beer bottles, or swing-top bottles. Standard wine bottles are not rated for carbonation pressure and should not be used.

## Keeving

Keeving is a traditional method for producing naturally sweet sparkling cider without back-sweetening or stabilization. It's a technique specific to cider (particularly Breton and Normandy cider traditions) and worth knowing about even if you don't attempt it immediately.

The principle is to deliberately deplete the must of nutrients before fermentation so that the yeast, lacking what they need to complete fermentation, naturally stall at a low alcohol level with residual sugar intact. The nutrient depletion is achieved by encouraging a gel (called a chapeau brun, or brown cap) to form on the surface of the juice shortly after pressing, which traps proteins and nutrients as it rises. The clarified juice beneath is then drawn off and fermented slowly at cool temperatures.

A successful keeved cider finishes at 3–5% ABV with significant residual sugar and natural carbonation from the slow, incomplete fermentation — complex, gently sparkling, and genuinely delicious. It requires specific apple varieties (high in pectin and calcium, which support cap formation), cool temperatures, and patience. Not a first project, but a beautiful goal for when you're ready for it.

## Perry

Perry — fermented pear juice — follows the same process as cider and deserves a brief mention here. True perry made from perry pear varieties (Blakeney Red, Moorcroft, Barland) is one of the most distinctive and underappreciated fermented drinks in the world — delicate, complex, and unlike anything else. It's rare because perry pear trees take decades to reach full production and the varieties are mostly grown in a small region of England and Wales.

Perry made from regular dessert pears is more accessible and produces a pleasant, soft drink, though it lacks the complexity of true perry. If you have access to any old pear trees of unknown variety, they're worth fermenting experimentally — old heritage varieties often turn out to be perry pears that have been growing unrecognized for generations.
