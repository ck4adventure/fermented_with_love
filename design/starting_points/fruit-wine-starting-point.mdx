# Fruit wine starting point

This is a framework for a one-gallon wild fruit wine. One gallon is the right scale to start — enough to learn from, small enough that a batch that doesn't work out isn't a catastrophe. Once you've made a few gallons and understand how your particular fruit and environment behave, scaling up is straightforward.

Everything here is a starting point, not a prescription. Read the ratio, understand why it exists, then look at what you have and adjust accordingly.

---

## What you need

**Fruit:** 3–5 lbs per gallon, depending on the fruit. Denser, more flavorful fruit (plums, blackberries, elderberries) toward the lower end. Lighter, more watery fruit (strawberries, rhubarb) toward the higher end. More fruit means more flavor extraction and more natural sugar; less means a more delicate wine that leans harder on the sugar base.

**Sugar:** Enough to bring your must to a starting gravity of 1.085–1.095, which will give you approximately 11–12% ABV when fermented to dryness. Your hydrometer reading after crushing the fruit tells you how much sugar the fruit is already contributing — subtract that from your target and make up the difference. See the [sugar additions page](/working-with-fruit/sugar-additions) for the calculation.

**Water:** Enough to bring the total volume to one gallon after the fruit is added. Some fruits (stone fruit, berries) release a lot of juice and need less added water. Others (rhubarb, citrus) need more.

**Acid:** Most fruits provide enough natural acidity. If the must tastes flat or the fruit is very ripe and low in acid, add a small amount of acid blend — a quarter teaspoon per gallon to start, taste, and adjust.

**Tannin (optional):** A quarter teaspoon of tannin powder, or half a cup of strong unsweetened black tea, adds structure that many fruit wines benefit from — especially if you're not fermenting on skins.

**Yeast nutrients (optional but recommended):** A quarter teaspoon of Fermaid-O or similar, added after fermentation is clearly underway, reduces the risk of a stressed wild fermentation producing off-flavors.

## The process

### Day 0: prepare the must

Crush or chop your fruit into a clean, wide-mouth fermentation vessel — a large mason jar or food-grade bucket. Include the skins for stone fruit and berries; they contribute color, flavor, and wild yeast. Remove pits from stone fruit if you can (cherry pits especially release bitterness with extended contact).

Take a hydrometer reading of the juice that releases from the crushed fruit. Calculate how much sugar you need to reach your target gravity (1.085–1.095). Dissolve the sugar in a small amount of warm water, let it cool, and stir it into the must. Take another reading to confirm you've hit your target.

Cover the vessel loosely — a cloth, a loose lid, a piece of plastic wrap with a few small holes. You want CO2 to escape freely without letting insects in.

Leave at room temperature (65–75°F is ideal). Do not add commercial yeast unless your fruit is from a source with poor wild yeast populations (heavily treated supermarket fruit), in which case pitch a small amount of wine yeast now.

### Days 1–3: waiting for activity

Wild fermentation takes longer to get going than commercial yeast. In the first 24 hours, you may see nothing. By 48–72 hours in good conditions, you should see small bubbles beginning to form, the must starting to smell yeasty and alive, and the fruit solids beginning to rise.

If you're not seeing activity by day four in a warm environment, stir vigorously and wait another 24 hours. If still nothing by day five, consider pitching a small amount of commercial yeast to rescue the batch.

### Days 1–7: primary fermentation (active phase)

Stir or punch down the fruit cap at least once daily — twice is better. This keeps the cap moist, prevents surface mold, redistributes yeast, and gives you daily sensory contact with the ferment. Taste a small amount each time. Smell it. The ferment changes noticeably day by day during this phase.

If you're adding yeast nutrients, add a small amount (quarter teaspoon per gallon) on day two or three, once fermentation is clearly active.

Take a hydrometer reading every few days to track the gravity dropping. Active primary fermentation typically drops gravity by 0.005–0.015 per day at its peak.

### Days 7–21: primary fermentation (quieting)

Bubbling slows. The cap rises less aggressively between punchdowns and eventually stops rising. The must starts to smell less aggressively yeasty and more like young wine. Take a gravity reading — when it's dropped below 1.020 and the cap is no longer rising, you're ready to rack.

The timing varies considerably with wild fermentation. Don't rush it. A ferment that's still slowly dropping gravity at two weeks is still working and should be left alone.

### Racking to secondary

Strain the must through a fine mesh strainer or muslin cloth into a clean one-gallon glass jug. Press the fruit gently to extract remaining liquid. Fit a bung and airlock. The jug should be as full as possible — top up with a small amount of water if needed to minimize headspace.

Take a gravity reading. You're probably somewhere between 1.010 and 1.030 at this point for a typical wild fermentation. Fermentation will continue slowly in the sealed vessel.

For a detailed walkthrough of racking technique, see the [racking page](/fermentation/racking).

### Weeks 3–8: secondary fermentation

The airlock bubbles slowly — every few minutes at first, tapering to every ten or fifteen minutes, then stopping. Sediment accumulates at the bottom of the jug. The wine begins to clear from the top down.

Rack again when you see a quarter inch or more of sediment at the bottom, or every four to six weeks. Each racking clarifies the wine and removes material that would otherwise affect flavor.

Take gravity readings every one to two weeks. Fermentation is complete when you get the same reading two days in a row.

### Confirming completion and tasting

When gravity has been stable for two consecutive readings — typically somewhere between 0.995 and 1.005 for a dry fruit wine — fermentation is done. Taste the wine. Ask yourself:

- Does it taste like the fruit it came from?
- Is there enough acidity to make it feel alive, or does it taste flat?
- Is the sweetness level where you want it, or would it benefit from being slightly sweeter or drier?
- Does it taste complete, or does it still taste rough and unresolved?

If it tastes rough and unresolved, it probably needs more time — either in bulk aging in the vessel or in bottle. This is normal for young wine, especially wild-fermented wine that has had more microbial activity than a single commercial yeast strain.

### Bottling

When the wine is clear, stable, and tasting the way you want it — or close enough — bottle it. For a still wine, standard bottles with corks or swing-tops work well. For a lightly sparkling wine, make sure you're using pressure-rated bottles.

For the full bottling walkthrough, see the [bottling page](/fermentation/bottling).

## Approximate timeline

| Stage | Duration |
|---|---|
| Preparing must | Day 0 |
| Waiting for wild fermentation to start | Days 1–3 |
| Active primary fermentation | Days 3–14 |
| Racking to secondary | Days 10–21 |
| Secondary fermentation | Weeks 3–8 |
| Clearing and aging | 1–4 months |
| Bottling | 2–6 months from start |
| Drinking | Any time after bottling; better after 1–3 months in bottle |

Wild ferments sit at the longer end of these ranges. Commercial-yeast ferments move faster. Both produce good wine.

## Variations worth trying once you have the framework

**Ferment on skins longer or shorter.** More skin contact (up to a week in primary) extracts more tannin, color, and flavor. Less (two to three days) produces a lighter, more delicate wine. The right amount depends on the fruit and what you want from the finished wine.

**Blend two fruits.** A proportion of high-acid fruit alongside a sweeter, lower-acid fruit often produces more interesting results than either alone. Plum and apple. Blackberry and elderberry. Lemon and peach. Let taste guide the ratio.

**Leave more residual sweetness.** If you prefer semi-sweet wine, bottle when gravity is around 1.008–1.015 rather than waiting for full dryness — using pressure-rated bottles to handle any remaining fermentation activity.

**Extend bulk aging.** A wine that tastes young and rough at two months may taste genuinely good at four. Patience is always a legitimate intervention.
