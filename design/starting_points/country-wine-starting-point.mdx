# Country wine starting point

Country wine has a different structure from fruit wine and mead, and understanding that structure is the key to adapting it to whatever you're working with. In a fruit wine, the fruit provides flavor, sugar, acid, and wild yeast — it's doing most of the work. In a country wine, you're usually separating these functions. The flavoring ingredient provides flavor and sometimes wild yeast. Sugar, water, and additions provide everything else.

This separation is what makes the category so flexible. Once you understand the framework, you can apply it to flowers, vegetables, herbs, roots, or almost anything else with flavor worth capturing.

---

## The framework

A country wine at one gallon has five components:

**The flavoring ingredient.** This is what gives the wine its character. Flowers, herbs, and aromatic ingredients are typically used in smaller quantities — a cup or two of petals, a handful of herbs. Vegetables and roots with more diffuse flavor are used in larger quantities — several pounds. The right amount is the amount that produces the flavor intensity you want in the finished wine, which you'll calibrate over batches.

**A sugar and water base.** Unlike fruit wine, most country wine ingredients don't contain meaningful fermentable sugar. Sugar dissolved in water provides the fermentable substrate. Target a starting gravity of 1.080–1.095 for a wine at 11–12% ABV — the same target as fruit wine. The amount of sugar needed is roughly 2–2.5 lbs per gallon.

**Acid.** Most country wine ingredients are low in acid. Some addition is almost always needed — lemon juice or citric acid is traditional and works well. Acid blend gives a cleaner, less citrus-forward result. A quarter teaspoon of acid blend per gallon, or the juice of one lemon per gallon, is a reasonable starting point. Taste and adjust.

**Tannin.** Also typically absent from country wine ingredients. A small addition adds structure that most country wines need — without it the wine can taste flat and one-dimensional regardless of how good the flavoring ingredient is. A quarter teaspoon of tannin powder or half a cup of strong unsweetened black tea per gallon is sufficient.

**Yeast and nutrients.** Wild if your flavoring ingredient carries a viable wild yeast population (elderflowers often do; most vegetables don't). Commercial wine yeast otherwise — 71B works well for delicate flower wines; EC-1118 for more robust vegetable wines. Add yeast nutrients (a quarter teaspoon of Fermaid-O per gallon, staggered over the first week of fermentation) regardless of which yeast you use, since the must is nutrient-poor.

## Extracting flavor from the ingredient

How you get flavor from your ingredient into the must depends on what the ingredient is.

**Cold infusion (flowers and delicate aromatics):** Steep the ingredient in cold or room-temperature water for 24–48 hours, then strain out the solids and use the infused water as your must base. Preserves volatile aromatic compounds that heat would destroy. Best for elderflowers, rose petals, lavender, jasmine, and anything where the fragrance is the point.

**Hot infusion (hardier herbs, roots, spices):** Steep in near-boiling water for 20–30 minutes, then cool completely before adding yeast. Extracts flavor from ingredients that don't give up their character easily in cold water — ginger root, dried herbs, oak bark, cinnamon. Kills wild yeast on the ingredient, so you'll need to add commercial yeast.

**Cooking and pressing (vegetables):** Most vegetables need to be cooked to soften and release their flavor compounds — simmered in water until tender, then the liquid strained off and used as the must base. Rhubarb is typically sliced and steeped in hot water rather than cooked until fully soft.

**Direct fermentation (some fruits used as country wine):** Some ingredients — raisins, dried fruit, citrus peel — can go directly into the must and be fermented on, then strained off. Raisins in particular are a common addition to country wines as a flavor and nutrient supplement.

For flowers and delicate aromatics, a **second addition** in secondary fermentation — a small amount of fresh ingredient added after primary fermentation has quieted — restores aromatic brightness that fermentation stripped away. Remove it after two to four days by tasting — when the flavor is right, rack off the ingredient.

## The process

### Day 0: prepare the must

Extract flavor from your ingredient using whichever method suits it (see above). Strain out all solids. Measure the liquid into your fermentation vessel and top up to one gallon with water.

Dissolve sugar in a small amount of warm water, let it cool, and add to the must. Take a hydrometer reading and adjust to reach your target gravity (1.080–1.095). Add acid and tannin. Stir thoroughly.

If going wild and your ingredient was cold-infused (preserving wild yeast), cover loosely and leave at room temperature. If using commercial yeast, or if the ingredient was heated (killing wild yeast), pitch commercial yeast now.

### Days 1–14: primary fermentation

Country wine primary fermentation follows the same pattern as fruit wine, with one difference: there's no fruit cap to punch down. Stir once daily to keep yeast in suspension and degas the must. Add yeast nutrients in staggered amounts (quarter teaspoon per gallon) on days two, four, and six of active fermentation.

Track gravity. When it drops below 1.020 and activity is clearly slowing, rack to a clean secondary vessel with an airlock.

### Weeks 2–8: secondary fermentation and clearing

Country wines clear relatively quickly because there are no fruit solids, pectin, or skin tannins to complicate clarification. A country wine may be notably clear within four to six weeks of racking to secondary.

Rack when you see significant sediment accumulation. Taste at each racking — the flavor evolves considerably during this phase, and tasting is the best way to know when the wine is ready to bottle.

### Adding botanicals in secondary (optional)

If you want to refresh the aromatic character of the wine — particularly relevant for flower wines — add a small amount of the fresh ingredient to the secondary vessel now. A small handful of flowers, a few sprigs of fresh herb. Taste daily and rack off the ingredient when the flavor is where you want it. This step is optional but often makes a significant difference in delicate flower wines.

### Confirming completion and bottling

Same as fruit wine: two identical gravity readings two days apart, typically at or below 1.000 for a dry country wine. Bottle in appropriate bottles — still wine in standard bottles with corks or swing-tops, sparkling in pressure-rated bottles.

Country wines are generally best drunk relatively young — within one to two years of bottling — before the delicate aromatics fade. Vegetable wines with more body and structure (parsnip, root vegetable wines) can sometimes age longer.

|---|---|

| Preparing must | Day 0 |

| Racking to secondary | Weeks 2–4 |
| Secondary fermentation | Weeks 3–8 |
| Optional secondary botanical addition | Weeks 4–6 |
| Clearing | Weeks 4–10 |
| Bottling | 2–4 months from start |
| Drinking | Any time after bottling; best within 1–2 years |

## Calibrating the flavoring ingredient

The hardest variable in country winemaking is how much of the ingredient to use. Too little and the flavor is imperceptible in the finished wine. Too much and it overwhelms everything else — lavender becomes medicinal, elderflower becomes perfumed, ginger becomes harsh.

A few general orientations:

**Flowers:** 1–2 cups of petals per gallon for delicate flowers (rose, lavender); 2–4 cups for more robust ones (elderflower, dandelion). These are starting points — expect to adjust based on what you taste.

**Aromatic herbs:** Small quantities. A few sprigs of fresh lemon verbena, rosemary, or thyme per gallon in primary, with a secondary addition to taste. Err well on the side of too little until you understand how strongly a particular herb comes through in fermentation.

**Vegetables and roots:** 3–5 lbs per gallon of most vegetables. Ginger is the exception — it's intensely flavored and 4–6 oz of fresh grated ginger per gallon is usually plenty.

Document exactly what you used and for how long in every batch. Country winemaking is highly iterative — the second batch of any given ingredient is almost always better than the first, because you know what to adjust.
