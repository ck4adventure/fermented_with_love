# Mead starting point

Mead is slower than fruit wine. That's the first thing to know, and it's worth sitting with before you start, because the instincts you've built from making fruit wine will tell you something is wrong when it isn't. A wild mead that takes five days to show any activity is not failing. A mead that's still slowly bubbling at two months is not stuck — it's just being a mead.

The slowness is partly because honey is nutrient-poor, which means yeast work harder and more gradually than they do in a fruit must. It's partly because the flavors that make good mead take time to emerge and integrate. And it's partly just the nature of the substrate — honey is dense, complex, and doesn't give up its character quickly. Give it the time it needs.

This framework is for a traditional still mead — honey, water, yeast, nutrients — at one gallon. It's the simplest version and the right starting point. Once you understand how honey ferments and what good mead tastes like at each stage, the variations (fruit, herbs, spices) follow naturally.

---

## What you need

**Honey:** 2.5–3 lbs per gallon. This gives a starting gravity of roughly 1.090–1.110 and a finished ABV of approximately 12–14% when fermented to dryness. Use the best honey you can find — local, raw, and ideally from a beekeeper who can tell you what the bees were foraging on. The honey is everything in a traditional mead; this is not a place to use the supermarket clover honey that's been sitting in the cupboard.

**Water:** Enough to bring the total volume to one gallon after the honey is dissolved. Room temperature or slightly warm — not hot, which would destroy aromatic compounds in the honey and kill any wild yeast present.

**Yeast:** Wild if your honey is genuinely raw and local, or if you want to experiment. Commercial yeast (71B is the most recommended for mead — it preserves honey character and produces pleasant esters) if you want reliable results. See the [mead page](/beyond-fruit-wine/mead) for more on this choice.

**Yeast nutrients:** Non-optional. This is the most important difference from fruit wine. Honey contains almost no yeast-assimilable nitrogen, and without nutrient additions your fermentation will be slow, stressed, and prone to producing hydrogen sulfide. Use Fermaid-O or a similar organic nutrient blend. You'll add it in stages, not all at once.

**Acid:** Honey is typically low in acid. A small addition — a quarter teaspoon of acid blend or the juice of half a lemon per gallon — improves both fermentation and the flavor of the finished mead. Traditional meads were often made without acid additions, which is part of why they have a reputation for tasting flat to modern palates. A small addition makes a meaningful difference.

## The process

### Day 0: prepare the must

Dissolve the honey in water. Don't heat it — room temperature water and patience (and stirring) will dissolve honey completely without destroying its aromatics. If the honey is very thick and resistant, warm the water to no more than 100°F before adding the honey, then let it cool before doing anything else.

Once dissolved, take a hydrometer reading. Target 1.090–1.110. Add more honey to raise the gravity, more water to lower it.

Add your acid addition if using. Do not add nutrients yet — you'll add those after fermentation begins.

If using commercial yeast, rehydrate and pitch now per package instructions. If going wild, cover loosely and leave at room temperature (70–75°F is better for wild mead than the cooler end of the range).

### Days 1–5: waiting for activity

Wild mead is slow to start. Don't expect to see activity in the first 24–48 hours. By day three to five in good conditions you should see small bubbles, a faint yeasty smell, and possibly a slight foam on the surface. If you see nothing by day six, stir vigorously to introduce some oxygen, warm the must slightly if possible, and wait another 48 hours. If still nothing, pitch a small amount of commercial yeast.

### Days 2–10: staggered nutrient additions

This is the most important management technique in mead fermentation. Once fermentation is clearly active — visible bubbles, the must smelling yeasty and alive — begin your nutrient additions.

Add a quarter teaspoon of Fermaid-O per gallon on day two or three (once fermentation is established), again on day four or five, and once more on day seven or eight. Three small additions across the first week of active fermentation is sufficient for a one-gallon batch. Stir gently after each addition.

Do not add nutrients all at once at the start. Do not add them after the gravity has dropped below 1.030 — at that point the yeast are well-established and the window for nutrient benefit has largely passed.

### Weeks 1–4: primary fermentation

Mead ferments more slowly than fruit wine even with good nutrients. Expect primary fermentation to take two to four weeks for commercial yeast, and potentially longer for wild. Track gravity every few days. Active fermentation typically drops gravity by 0.005–0.010 per day at its peak — slower than fruit wine, and the pace will be uneven.

You don't have a fruit cap to punch down, so daily interaction is less structured than with fruit wine. Swirl the vessel gently once daily to keep the yeast in suspension and prevent them from settling and going dormant before fermentation is complete.

Taste periodically. Young mead at this stage tastes sweet, slightly yeasty, and not particularly interesting — the character of the honey is still largely transformed by active fermentation. This is normal.

### Racking to secondary

When gravity has dropped below 1.020 and activity is clearly slowing, rack to a clean secondary vessel fitted with a bung and airlock. Minimize headspace — top up with a small amount of water or a similar mead if needed. The mead will look completely opaque and slightly tan or golden at this point.

### Months 1–3: secondary fermentation and early clearing

Fermentation continues slowly. The mead begins to clear from the top down. Sediment accumulates. Rack again when you see a quarter inch of sediment at the bottom.

Taste at each racking. The mead is transforming — the aggressive yeasty quality of primary fermentation softens, and the honey character begins to re-emerge. At one month it may still taste rough and young. At two months it should be more recognizably mead-like.

### Months 3–6: clearing, aging, and the patience phase

This is where mead distinguishes itself from fruit wine in terms of timeline. A mead that's been fermenting for three months may still have another two or three months of clearing and flavor development ahead of it before it's ready to bottle.

Take gravity readings monthly. When you've had two identical readings a month apart — not two days apart as with fruit wine, because mead ferments so slowly that two days doesn't tell you much — fermentation is complete.

The mead should be noticeably clear by month three or four. If it's still hazy, bentonite (a quarter teaspoon per gallon dissolved in warm water and stirred in gently) will accelerate clarification. Cold crashing for a week also helps significantly.

### Bottling and bottle aging

Bottle when the mead is clear, stable, and gravity has been confirmed complete. Standard wine bottles with corks work well for still mead. For sparkling mead, use champagne bottles or crown-capped beer bottles.

Mead continues to improve in bottle, often for a year or more. A mead that tastes thin or slightly harsh at bottling may taste genuinely good at six months and excellent at a year. This is not a failure of the mead — it's the nature of the product.

## Approximate timeline

| Stage | Duration |
|---|---|
| Preparing must | Day 0 |
| Waiting for fermentation to start (wild) | Days 1–5 |
| Active primary fermentation | Weeks 1–4 |
| Racking to secondary | Weeks 3–5 |
| Secondary fermentation | Months 1–3 |
| Clearing and bulk aging | Months 2–6 |
| Bottling | Months 4–8 from start |
| Drinking | Bottling day onward; notably better at 6–12 months |

## Getting to something drinkable sooner

The timeline above is for a mead worth aging. But there's real value in getting something into a glass earlier — the feedback loop of tasting your own mead is how instincts develop, and a six-month wait between batch and feedback is a long time.

A few ways to shorten the path to a drinkable result without sacrificing too much quality:

**Make a lower-gravity session mead.** Target a starting gravity of 1.060–1.070 rather than 1.090–1.110. This gives you a finished mead around 7–9% ABV — lower alcohol, lighter body, and a shorter fermentation timeline. It won't age as well or develop as much complexity, but it ferments and clears faster, and the honey character is often more vivid in lower-alcohol meads because there's less alcohol masking it. A session mead can be drinkable in six to eight weeks rather than four to six months.

**Use commercial yeast.** Wild mead is interesting but slow. 71B fermented mead with good nutrient additions can be drinkable in eight to ten weeks from the start. Make one wild batch for the experience; make a commercial-yeast batch alongside it for comparison and for something to drink while you wait.

**Don't wait for perfect clarity.** A slightly hazy mead that's fermentation-complete and flavor-stable is completely safe and often still delicious. Clarity is aesthetic. If the mead tastes good and you want to taste it, taste it.

The goal of the first batch isn't a perfect mead. It's understanding what honey ferments like, what young mead tastes like, and what changes with time. That knowledge is the foundation for everything you make afterward.
