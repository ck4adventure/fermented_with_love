# How to use these pages

A recipe tells you exactly what to do. A framework tells you how to think.

The pages in this section are frameworks. They give you ratios, timelines, and decision points — enough structure to get started without enough rigidity to stop you from adapting to what's actually in front of you. The fruit you have, the equipment you own, the season you're in.

The reason for this isn't philosophical stubbornness. It's that fermentation can't be fully scripted. A recipe written for a 1.055 plum must doesn't know that your plums read 1.042. A recipe with a two-week primary timeline doesn't know that your wild ferment took three weeks to get going and is still working. If you follow the recipe anyway, you get worse results than if you'd been paying attention and responded to what the ferment was actually doing.

What develops through that kind of attention — tasting, reading, adjusting — is instinct. And instinct is more useful than any recipe, because it travels with you into every batch you'll ever make, including the ones that don't resemble anything you've made before.

So use these pages to get oriented. Read the ratio, understand why it's that ratio, then look at your actual ingredients and adjust. Follow the timeline as a rough guide, but follow your hydrometer and your nose more closely. Make something, taste it, note what you'd do differently, and make it again.

The learning is in the doing. These pages just give you somewhere to start.
