# Bottling

Bottling is the moment the project becomes real — when wine that's been sitting in a carboy for weeks or months transforms into something you can hand to someone, store on a shelf, or open on an occasion. It's satisfying in a way that the intermediate stages aren't.

It's also the stage where some easily avoided mistakes can ruin a batch you've invested a lot of time in. The main ones are bottling too early (before fermentation is truly complete) and not thinking through whether you want still or sparkling wine before you seal anything.

## Confirming fermentation is finished

This is the most important step before you touch a bottle. Bottling wine that still has active yeast and residual sugar is how you end up with bottles that overcarbonate and either push their corks out or — in worst cases with sealed bottles — build enough pressure to break the glass.

Fermentation is complete when:
- You get the same hydrometer reading two days in a row (usually around 1.000 or below for a dry wine, higher if residual sweetness was your goal)
- Bubbling through the airlock has stopped entirely
- The wine has cleared noticeably, with sediment settled at the bottom

Don't rely on a single stopped-bubbling reading. Wild ferments in particular can appear dormant, restart slowly, and fool you. Two identical gravity readings, 48 hours apart, is the reliable confirmation.

## Still wine vs sparkling

Before you bottle, decide what you're making. This decision affects everything that follows.

**Still wine** is the simpler case. Fermentation is complete, the wine has no intention of producing more CO2, and you're simply moving it into bottles for storage and aging. Standard wine bottles with corks, or swing-top bottles with rubber gaskets, work well here.

**Sparkling wine** is deliberately bottled while a small amount of fermentable sugar remains — or with a small addition of sugar at bottling time — so that a secondary fermentation happens inside the sealed bottle, producing CO2 that dissolves into the wine and creates carbonation. This is how traditional sparkling wines are made, and the principle is exactly the same for fruit wines.

Sparkling wine requires bottles and closures rated for pressure. Standard wine bottles are not rated for pressure and should never be used for actively sparkling wine. Use:

- **Champagne-style bottles** (heavy, thick glass with a punt at the bottom) with mushroom corks and wire cages, or
- **Crown caps** (the same caps used on beer bottles) applied with a capper, which are inexpensive, reliable, and easy for small batches, or
- **Swing-top (Grolsch-style) bottles**, which are rated for moderate carbonation and very convenient for fruit wines

For a gently sparkling wine rather than fully effervescent, aim for around 2–3 volumes of CO2. The amount of priming sugar needed to achieve this depends on the temperature of the wine and its current carbonation level, but as a rough starting point for most fruit wines at cellar temperature: about 3/4 oz (22g) of sugar per gallon of wine will produce moderate carbonation.

Dissolve the sugar in a tiny amount of warm water, stir it gently into the wine before bottling (don't splash — oxidation risk), then bottle immediately. The sugar will be consumed by any remaining yeast, producing CO2 inside the sealed bottle.

## Equipment for bottling

**Bottles:** For still wine, standard 750ml wine bottles are the classic choice. For sparkling, champagne bottles or swing-tops. Wash and sanitize all bottles thoroughly — any residue or wild microorganism in the bottle will affect the finished wine.

**A siphon:** The same setup you used for racking. You'll siphon the wine from the carboy into the bottles, stopping just short of the sediment at the bottom of the carboy.

**A bottle filler wand** (optional but excellent): This is a rigid tube with a spring-loaded valve at the tip. Attach it to your siphon hose, insert it into the bottle, and wine flows only when the wand is pressed against the bottle bottom — filling from the bottom up with no splashing or oxidation. When you pull the wand out, it leaves the perfect headspace. Highly recommended.

**Corks or caps:** Corks require a corker (a hand-lever corker costs around $20 and works fine for home use). Crown caps require a capper. Swing-top bottles need no additional tools.

## How to bottle

1. Set up your carboy elevated above your workspace. Attach the siphon.

2. Fill each bottle from the bottom up, using a bottle filler wand if you have one. Leave about an inch of headspace for a still wine sealed with a cork (the cork will compress into this space). For crown-cap or swing-top bottles, leave slightly less.

3. Cork or cap each bottle immediately after filling. If you're corking, sanitize the corks first and use a corker rather than trying to push them in by hand.

4. Label each bottle. At minimum: the fruit, the batch (if you're running multiple), the date bottled, and a rough ABV if you calculated it. You'll thank yourself later.

5. Stand still wine bottles upright for 24–48 hours after corking to let any displaced air reabsorb, then store on their sides (cork-up storage dries corks out and lets air in over time). Swing-top and crown-capped bottles can go on their sides immediately.

## Storing after bottling

Still fruit wines with an ABV of 11%+ can be stored at room temperature in a cool, dark place. Light and heat both degrade wine over time — a basement, a cupboard away from the stove, or a proper wine rack in a temperate space all work well.

Sparkling wines should be stored upright so the sediment from secondary fermentation settles at the bottom, and should be drunk sooner rather than later — carbonation in home-made sparkling wines isn't as controlled as commercial products, and very long storage can lead to overcarbonation.

Wild fruit wines are generally best within 1–2 years of bottling. Unlike commercial wines, they often don't have the tannin structure or pH precision to benefit from very long aging. But there are exceptions. The only way to know is to open a bottle periodically and see.

## Tasting notes and patience

The wine you taste immediately after bottling is not the wine you'll be drinking in six months. Bottling introduces a small amount of oxygen into the wine, which temporarily changes the flavor profile — what's called **bottle shock**. It typically resolves within 2–4 weeks.

After that, the wine will continue to evolve slowly. Wild fruit wines often tighten up a bit in the first month and then open back out with complexity that wasn't there at bottling. Tasting a bottle at 1 month, 3 months, and 6 months is genuinely interesting, and it teaches you things about your own fermentations that no amount of reading will.
