# Troubleshooting

Most fermentations proceed without drama. But things do go wrong sometimes, and when they do, knowing what you're looking at — and whether it actually requires intervention — is worth a lot more than panicking and either doing nothing or doing too much.

The first and most important principle: smell and taste are your best diagnostic tools. A ferment that smells and tastes good, even if it looks weird, is probably fine. A ferment that smells wrong even if it looks fine is telling you something important.

## Fermentation won't start

**What it looks like:** No bubbles, no CO2 through the airlock, no visible activity after 48–72 hours at room temperature.

**Likely causes:**
- Temperature too cold. Wild yeast slow dramatically below 60°F and may not start at all below 55°F. Move the vessel somewhere warmer and wait another 24 hours.
- Low natural yeast population on the fruit. This is common with supermarket fruit treated with fungicides. The surface yeast simply aren't there in numbers large enough to establish fermentation.
- Very high sugar concentration stressing the yeast osmotically before they've gotten started.
- Airlock problem: sometimes the airlock is bubbling and you can't see it, or CO2 is escaping around the bung rather than through the airlock. Check the seal before concluding fermentation hasn't started.

**What to do:** First rule out temperature and seal issues. If neither fixes it after 72 hours, stir the must vigorously to introduce a little oxygen and redistribute anything that's settled, and move to a warmer spot. If you still see nothing after another 48 hours, you can pitch a small amount of commercial yeast. This isn't giving up on wild fermentation — it's rescuing the batch. The commercial yeast will get things going and the wild character already present in the must will still contribute.

## Fermentation stalls partway through

**What it looks like:** Bubbling slows or stops while the hydrometer reading is still well above 1.000 (usually above 1.010). The wine is not done fermenting but the yeast appear to have quit.

**Likely causes:**
- Yeast tapped out due to alcohol level. Wild strains sometimes stop around 8–10% ABV if they've exhausted their tolerance.
- Nutrient deficiency causing yeast stress and early dropout.
- Temperature drop: a sudden cold snap or moving the vessel somewhere cooler can pause fermentation that looked active.
- Very high starting gravity that osmotically stressed the yeast from the beginning.

**What to do:** First, check temperature. If the vessel has gotten cold, bring it somewhere warmer and wait — stuck fermentations often restart on their own with a few degrees of warming. Gently stir the must to rouse any yeast that have settled. If you want to push ABV higher, pitch a robust commercial strain like EC-1118 — it can usually get a stalled ferment moving again. Adding a small amount of yeast nutrient at this stage can also help if nutrient deficiency is the likely cause.

## Rotten egg or sulfur smell (hydrogen sulfide)

**What it looks like:** The ferment smells sharply of rotten eggs or struck matches. The wine itself may look fine.

**What's happening:** Yeast under nutrient stress — particularly nitrogen deficiency — produce hydrogen sulfide (H₂S) as a byproduct. It's a stress response, not a sign that the batch is ruined.

**What to do:** Act quickly, because H₂S can bond with other compounds in the wine and become much harder to remove if you wait. Splash the must: pour it from one vessel to another several times, or stir it vigorously with a whisk. The hydrogen sulfide is a volatile gas and will dissipate into the air with agitation. Do this outdoors or in a well-ventilated space.

If the smell returns after splashing, add yeast nutrient and splash again. In mild cases, H₂S clears entirely on its own by the time the wine has finished fermenting and racked once or twice. In severe cases, a small amount of copper can help (some winemakers stir with a clean copper spoon), though this is an advanced intervention worth researching before trying.

Don't bottle wine that still smells of sulfur.

## Vinegar smell (acetic acid)

**What it looks like:** The wine smells sharply of vinegar or has a sour, acidic bite that's more pungent than tart.

**What's happening:** *Acetobacter* bacteria have gotten into the ferment and are converting alcohol into acetic acid. Acetobacter needs oxygen to thrive, so it tends to establish at the surface — on a drying cap in primary fermentation, or in headspace during secondary.

**What to do:** Unfortunately, once significant acetic acid has developed, it can't easily be reversed. A slight vinegar edge can sometimes be masked by other flavors and may soften over time, but a wine that strongly smells of vinegar is not going to improve. If the acidity is mild, complete the fermentation, bottle it, and taste it in a few months — sometimes what seems sharp early on settles. If it's strong, accept the loss and learn from it.

Prevention is much more effective than treatment. During primary fermentation, punch down the cap at least once a day to prevent it from drying out. During secondary, minimize headspace and keep the airlock full of water so it doesn't dry out and allow air in.

## Surface mold on the cap

**What it looks like:** Gray, white, or occasionally blue-green fuzzy growth on the fruit cap during primary fermentation.

**What's happening:** The cap — fruit solids floating on the surface — dried out between punchdowns and provided a substrate for airborne mold spores to establish. This is very common and usually not catastrophic.

**What to do:** Carefully remove as much of the mold from the surface as you can without stirring it into the must. Then punch the cap down thoroughly. Increase your punchdown frequency to at least twice daily. The wine beneath the cap is almost always unaffected — the active fermentation environment is hostile to most molds, and any mold flavor compounds tend to stay in the cap rather than migrating into the liquid.

If the mold is extensive, on every surface, and the must smells strongly musty or off, that's a more serious situation. But a patch of surface mold on a cap that was left too long is routine.

## Wine won't clear

**What it looks like:** Weeks or months after fermentation finished, the wine is still hazy and doesn't seem to be clearing.

**Likely causes:**
- Fermentation isn't actually complete. A very slow, residual fermentation will keep fine particles in suspension. Confirm with two identical gravity readings 48 hours apart.
- Pectin haze. Fruit contains pectin, a natural gelling agent, and in wine it can form a stable haze that doesn't settle out on its own. This is common in apple and berry wines. Pectic enzyme added at the start of fermentation (or added now) breaks pectin down and allows the wine to clear.
- Protein haze. More common in mead and wines made with very ripe or overripe fruit. Bentonite clay, a fining agent, binds proteins and causes them to drop out of suspension.
- The wine just needs more time. Wild ferments often take longer to clear than commercial-yeast wines, because the diverse population of organisms takes longer to fully settle. Multiple rackings over several months will usually resolve this.

**What to do:** First confirm fermentation is complete. If it is, rack into a clean vessel, minimize headspace, and wait another 4–6 weeks. If there's no improvement after a second racking, consider pectic enzyme (for fruit wines) or bentonite (for protein hazes). Cold crashing — moving the vessel somewhere near-freezing for a week — can dramatically accelerate clarification by causing particles to clump and settle more quickly.

## Wine tastes thin or flat

**What it looks like:** The wine is clear and smells fine but it doesn't have much flavor, body, or complexity.

**What's happening:** This usually comes down to one of three things: the fruit didn't have much character to start with, the fermentation was too efficient (stripping rather than building flavor), or the wine needs more time to develop.

**What to do:** The first step is always waiting. Young fruit wines often taste flat or unformed immediately after fermentation, then open considerably in the first 3–6 months in bottle. If the wine has aged and still tastes thin, you can try blending — sometimes a wine that's weak on its own becomes more interesting when combined with a smaller amount of something with more character. A few ounces of a more flavorful wine or even a strongly flavored fruit juice, incorporated carefully, can round things out.

*Fermentation problems are how you learn. Every imperfect batch teaches you something a perfect batch doesn't.*
