# Fruit by type

Different fruits bring different strengths and challenges to fermentation. Some are naturally sugar-rich and need little help hitting a stable alcohol level. Others are low in fermentable sugar despite tasting sweet. Some carry exceptional wild yeast populations; others need a boost. Some produce vivid, aromatic wines that are best drunk young; others develop into something more complex with time.

This page is a practical reference — something to consult when you're planning a new batch or trying to understand why a particular ferment is behaving the way it is. OG ranges are approximate and vary with variety, ripeness, and growing conditions. Always take your own hydrometer reading.

## Stone fruit

### Plums

**Natural OG range:** 1.045–1.060
**Typical sugar addition needed:** Moderate to significant
**Acidity:** Moderate; varies by variety
**Wild yeast:** Excellent on foraged or garden fruit; the bloom on a ripe plum is one of the best natural yeast sources you'll find

Plum is one of the most rewarding fruits for wild fermentation, and one of the trickier ones to understand at first. The key thing to know is that plums contain significant amounts of sorbitol — a sugar alcohol that tastes sweet but cannot be fermented by wine yeast. This means plums often taste sweeter than their fermentable sugar content actually is, and the must will read lower on the hydrometer than you might expect from tasting the fruit. Plan for meaningful sugar additions to reach a stable ABV.

The finished wine is worth the effort. Plum wine develops a deep, complex character — rich, slightly tannic from the skins, with a stone fruit quality that's distinctly its own and becomes more interesting with age. It benefits from fermenting on the skins for several days during primary fermentation, and from aging for at least six months after bottling. A wine that tastes a bit austere at three months may taste genuinely lovely at a year.

Red and purple plum varieties produce wines with deep color and more tannin. Yellow and green plums produce lighter, more delicate wines with higher apparent acidity. Both work well; the finished character is quite different.

### Peaches and nectarines

**Natural OG range:** 1.050–1.075
**Typical sugar addition needed:** Little to moderate depending on ripeness
**Acidity:** Moderate; lower in very ripe fruit
**Wild yeast:** Good on tree-ripened fruit; poor on supermarket fruit

Ripe peaches ferment into a fragrant, soft wine with a pronounced stone fruit character. The aromatics are delicate and volatile — they're most vivid in young wine and fade with extended aging, so peach wine is generally best drunk within a year of bottling. Very ripe peaches produce low-acid must that can taste flat without an acid addition.

The fuzzy skin of peaches can contribute a slight bitterness if the fruit is fermented on the skins for too long. Two to three days of skin contact during primary fermentation is usually enough to extract color and flavor without the bitterness becoming dominant. Nectarines, being the same fruit without the fuzz, are easier to work with for skin-contact fermentation.

### Cherries

**Natural OG range:** 1.055–1.075
**Typical sugar addition needed:** Little; sweet varieties may need none
**Acidity:** High in sour varieties; moderate in sweet
**Wild yeast:** Good on farm-fresh fruit

Cherry wine divides cleanly by variety. Sour cherries (Morello, Montmorency) produce high-acid, deeply flavored wines with real complexity and good aging potential — they're among the most wine-like of fruit wines. Sweet cherries (Bing, Rainier) produce lower-acid wines with more obvious fruit sweetness but less structure.

Cherries should be pitted before fermentation or pressed carefully — cherry pits contain compounds that contribute an almond-like bitterness if left in contact with the must for too long. A light crush without breaking the pits, followed by a few days of skin contact and then pressing, extracts good color and flavor without the bitterness.

## Berries

### Blackberries and raspberries

**Natural OG range:** 1.040–1.060
**Typical sugar addition needed:** Moderate
**Acidity:** High; raspberries especially
**Wild yeast:** Variable; good on foraged wild berries, less reliable on farmed

Blackberry wine is a classic for good reason — it produces a deeply colored, full-flavored wine with genuine complexity and reasonable aging potential. Raspberry wine is more aromatic and lighter in body, with a distinctive tartness that can taste almost floral in the best batches. Both benefit from fermenting on the fruit for several days to extract full color and character.

Wild foraged blackberries often carry better yeast populations than farmed ones, and they tend to have more concentrated flavor from growing in full sun without irrigation. If you have access to foraged berries, use them.

The seeds in raspberries are numerous and very fine, which can make straining messy. A fine mesh straining bag during primary fermentation containing the fruit makes this much easier.

### Strawberries

**Natural OG range:** 1.030–1.050
**Typical sugar addition needed:** Significant
**Acidity:** Moderate
**Wild yeast:** Fair; better on local farm strawberries than supermarket

Strawberry wine is one of the more challenging berry wines to make well. The strawberry character is delicate and volatile — it fades faster during fermentation than most other berry aromatics, and the finished wine can taste of strawberry-adjacent fruit flavor rather than the vivid fresh strawberry you started with. Cold fermentation (if you can manage it) helps preserve the aromatics.

Strawberries are also lower in sugar and higher in water than most berries, so they need more sugar addition to reach a good ABV. Despite these challenges, a well-made strawberry wine has a delicate pink color and a subtle, lovely fruit character that's worth the effort.

### Elderberries

**Natural OG range:** 1.050–1.065
**Typical sugar addition needed:** Moderate
**Acidity:** High
**Wild yeast:** Excellent; elderberries carry exceptional wild yeast populations

Elderberry wine is intensely dark, tannic, and complex — one of the most wine-like of all fruit wines, with a character that genuinely rewards aging. The elderberry's high tannin content gives the wine structure that many fruit wines lack, and the finished wine can be cellared for several years with good results.

A note: raw elderberries contain compounds (primarily sambunigrin) that can cause nausea in quantity. The fermentation process neutralizes these, and cooked elderberries are completely safe — many winemakers briefly heat their elderberry must before fermenting. This does affect the wild yeast population, so you'd need to add commercial yeast or allow re-inoculation from the environment after cooling.

## Citrus

### Lemons

**Natural OG range:** 1.040–1.055 (juice only)
**Typical sugar addition needed:** Moderate to significant
**Acidity:** Very high
**Wild yeast:** Fair; best on unwaxed, untreated fruit

Lemon wine is one of the most surprising fruit wines for people who haven't tried it. It ferments into something crisp, bright, and intensely aromatic — more like a bone-dry citrus liqueur in character than most people expect. The acidity is high enough to produce a naturally lively, refreshing wine without any acid additions needed.

The aromatics are in the zest (the outer skin), not just the juice — including some zest in the must during primary fermentation adds considerable complexity and is worth doing. Be judicious with the white pith, which contributes bitterness. The finished wine is best drunk young and fresh, within a year of bottling, when the citrus aromatics are at their most vivid.

Wild fermentation of lemon wine works reasonably well because lemon juice's high acidity is actually hostile to most spoilage organisms, giving the wild yeast a relatively clean environment to establish in. Mint added to a sealed bottle of finished lemon wine will kick off a gentle secondary fermentation and add a lovely herbal dimension — a technique worth experimenting with.

### Oranges and grapefruits

**Natural OG range:** 1.045–1.060
**Typical sugar addition needed:** Moderate
**Acidity:** High; grapefruit especially
**Wild yeast:** Fair

Orange wine has a rounder, softer character than lemon — still bright and citrusy but with more body. Grapefruit wine is intensely bitter and aromatic, and benefits from blending with something sweeter and rounder to balance the bitterness. Both benefit from zest inclusion during primary fermentation.

## Apples and pears

### Apples

**Natural OG range:** 1.045–1.065
**Typical sugar addition needed:** Little to none for sessionable cider; moderate to reach 11–12% ABV
**Acidity:** Moderate; varies significantly by variety
**Wild yeast:** Excellent; apples carry some of the best wild yeast of any common fruit

Apple fermentation — cider — is in some ways the easiest introduction to wild fruit fermentation. Apples carry abundant wild yeast, have good natural acidity in most varieties, and ferment reliably and vigorously without much intervention. The main decision is how much sugar to add: for a fresh, sessionable cider at 6–8% ABV, little or none; for a shelf-stable wine-strength cider, additions to bring the OG to around 1.085.

Apple varieties make a significant difference in the finished character. High-tannin cider varieties (Kingston Black, Yarlington Mill, Dabinett) produce the most complex ciders but are rarely available outside specialist orchards. Culinary varieties (Bramley, Granny Smith) are high in acid and produce sharp, assertive ciders. Dessert varieties (Golden Delicious, Fuji) are lower in acid and produce rounder, less complex ciders that benefit from blending with something sharper.

If you have access to a mix of varieties, blending before fermentation produces better results than fermenting single varieties.

### Pears

**Natural OG range:** 1.045–1.060
**Typical sugar addition needed:** Little to moderate
**Acidity:** Lower than apples; pears are notably low-acid
**Wild yeast:** Good on tree-ripened fruit

Pear wine (perry) is softer and more delicate than cider, with a floral, slightly honeyed character. The low natural acidity of most pear varieties means acid additions are often needed to prevent the finished wine from tasting flat. True perry pears (specific cultivated varieties grown for the purpose) produce exceptional results; dessert pears produce pleasant if less complex wines.

## A note on blending

Some of the most interesting fruit wines aren't made from a single fruit but from combinations. High-acid fruits balance low-acid ones. Tannic fruits add structure to soft ones. Strong flavors complement or contrast with milder ones. Once you've made a few single-fruit wines and understand what each fruit contributes, blending either before or after fermentation opens up a much wider range of possibilities. Some combinations worth trying: plum and apple, blackberry and elderberry, lemon and ginger, peach and raspberry.
