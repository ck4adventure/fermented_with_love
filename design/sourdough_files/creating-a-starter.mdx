# Creating a sourdough starter

A sourdough starter isn't something you buy or import — it's something you cultivate from what's already there. Wild yeast and lactic acid bacteria are floating in the air around you, living on the surface of your flour, settled into the wood of your cutting board. The starter is just a method for concentrating and feeding them until you have a stable, predictable culture.

It takes about a week. Most of that time, you're just waiting.

## What you need

- **A jar**, at least 1 quart capacity. Wide-mouth mason jars work well. You want to be able to stir it easily and see what's happening inside.
- **Flour.** Whole wheat or whole rye flour for the first few days — the outer bran carries far more wild yeast than white flour does. You can transition to your preferred feeding flour once the starter is established.
- **Water.** If your tap water is heavily chlorinated, let it sit out for 30 minutes before using, or use filtered water. Chlorine can slow fermentation.
- **A loose cover.** A cloth secured with a rubber band, or a lid set on top without tightening. The culture needs airflow — it's catching yeast, not sealing it out.
- **A kitchen scale.** Baking by weight is more accurate and easier to calculate than volume, especially once you're scaling recipes.

That's it. No commercial yeast, no special equipment, no additives.

## The process, day by day

### Days 1–2: Starting the capture

Mix **50g whole wheat or rye flour** with **50g water** (around room temperature) in your jar. Stir vigorously for a minute — you're incorporating air and distributing everything evenly. Cover loosely and leave it somewhere warm. Around 70–75°F is ideal; a spot near your oven or on top of the fridge often works.

You're unlikely to see anything happen in the first 24 hours. That's fine.

### Days 3–4: Early activity

By day two or three, you may see small bubbles forming — a sign that microorganisms are waking up. The mixture might smell a little funky, sour, or almost alcoholic. This is normal and expected. What you're seeing isn't your eventual sourdough culture yet; it's often a first wave of bacteria that will later be outcompeted by the organisms you actually want.

**Discard and feed:** Remove all but about 50g of the mixture (the discard can go in the compost or, later, into pancakes and flatbreads). Add 50g flour and 50g water. Stir well, cover, and return to the same warm spot.

Repeat this once daily. You're not wasting flour — you're keeping the culture at a manageable size and giving the organisms a fresh food source before the old batch turns too acidic.

### Days 5–6: Things start to get interesting

By the middle of the week you should see more consistent bubbling, and the starter should be rising noticeably in the jar after feeding — sometimes doubling in size before falling back down. The smell shifts too: less sharp and funky, more yeasty and pleasantly sour, almost like beer or yogurt.

Continue once-daily discards and feedings with equal weights of flour and water.

If your kitchen is cool (below 68°F), things may move more slowly. That's not a failure — it just takes longer. Warmth accelerates fermentation; cold slows it. You can move the jar somewhere slightly warmer if you want to speed things up.

### Day 7: The float test

By the end of the week, a healthy starter should pass the **float test**: drop a small spoonful into a glass of water. If it floats, it's full of gas bubbles and strong enough to leaven bread. If it sinks, give it another day or two of feedings and try again.

A mature starter also has a predictable rhythm: it rises for several hours after feeding, peaks (doming slightly at the top, or showing a visible high-water mark on the jar if you've marked it), then slowly falls as the food runs out. Learning to read this rhythm is most of what sourdough baking is.

## What can go wrong

**Pink or orange streaks:** Discard the whole batch and start over. This is contamination, not wild yeast.

**A liquid layer on top:** This is called "hooch" — it's alcohol produced when the starter runs out of food. It smells sharp or boozy. Pour it off and feed the starter. It means you need to feed more frequently, or your starter is more active than your current schedule accommodates.

**No activity after a week:** Try moving it somewhere warmer, switching to a different flour with more bran, or using unfiltered tap water, which can sometimes contain trace minerals that help. Patience usually wins here — some environments just take longer.

**A strong, unpleasant smell (like nail polish remover or vomit):** Early starters often go through a stage that smells pretty bad. This typically resolves on its own within a few days as the beneficial organisms establish dominance. Keep feeding it and give it time before concluding something's wrong.

## How you'll know it's ready

A mature, ready-to-bake starter:

- Doubles (or more) in size within 4–8 hours of feeding
- Has a domed or slightly peaked surface at its highest point
- Smells tangy and yeasty — sour but pleasant
- Floats when dropped in water
- Has a web of bubbles visible through the jar

Once you're seeing this consistently for two or three days in a row, you have a stable culture. From here, you'll transition to a maintenance flour blend and a regular feeding schedule — which is covered in the next page.
