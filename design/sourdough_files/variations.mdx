# Additions and variations

The basic loaf is a platform. Once you understand how the dough behaves, you have a lot of room to move.

The general principle: inclusions and mix-ins usually don't change the core method, just the timing of when you add them. Flour swaps change hydration requirements and dough handling. Enrichments (fat, eggs, dairy) change structure and flavor in ways that open up a whole different category of bread.

Start with one change at a time so you know what you're adjusting to.

## Inclusions and mix-ins

These can be folded into the dough during bulk fermentation — add them during one of the later stretch-and-fold sets (the third or fourth), so the structure is already starting to develop before you disrupt it.

**Seeds:** Sesame, poppy, sunflower, flax, caraway, fennel, or pumpkin seeds add texture and flavor. Toast them first for more depth. They can go straight into the dough or be pressed onto the outside surface after shaping — roll the shaped loaf over a sprinkle of seeds on the counter.

**Herbs:** Fresh rosemary, thyme, or sage; dried oregano or herbes de Provence. Use fresh sparingly (they're stronger than they seem in a loaf) or simply go by smell.

**Olives:** Roughly chopped, well-drained kalamata or castelvetrano olives. Pat them dry before adding — extra moisture in the inclusions can make the dough slack and hard to shape. Olive and rosemary is a classic for good reason.

**Roasted garlic:** Squeeze an entire head of roasted garlic into the dough during the second or third fold. It disappears into the crumb and gives a mellow, buttery sweetness.

**Cheese:** Cubes of aged cheddar, gruyère, or manchego folded in before the final shaping. They melt during baking into pockets of richness. Finely grated parmesan pressed into the crust before baking gives a crackling, savory edge.

**Dried fruit and nuts:** Walnuts and dried cranberries, raisins and cinnamon, apricots and toasted almonds — this territory overlaps with enriched breads and works especially well with a higher whole wheat percentage. Soak very dry fruit in warm water for 10 minutes before adding.

**Caramelized onion:** Low and slow in a pan until golden and jammy. Cooled completely before adding to the dough. Rich and sweet — pairs naturally with thyme or blue cheese.

## Flour swaps

Every flour substitution changes how the dough feels and how much water it wants. Make changes gradually: swap out 10–20% at first, get a sense for the new behavior, then push further if you want.

**Higher whole wheat:** Increasing the whole wheat portion to 20–30% deepens the flavor and darkens the crumb. The dough will feel slightly stickier and may need a bit more water, or a slightly shorter bulk fermentation (whole wheat ferments faster). Above 40% whole wheat, the gluten network weakens and the loaf will be denser — still delicious, but different.

**Rye flour:** Even small amounts (10–15%) add a distinctive earthy, slightly tangy flavor and make the dough noticeably more extensible and slack. Rye absorbs water differently than wheat and the gluten behaves differently too — it's worth treating your first rye-addition loaf as an experiment rather than expecting the same handling as your standard recipe.

**Spelt:** A lighter, nuttier flavor than whole wheat. Spelt gluten is more fragile, so handle the dough gently during shaping — overworking can cause it to tear. Lovely for a mild, slightly sweet loaf.

**Einkorn or emmer:** Ancient grains with complex flavor and lower gluten than modern wheat. Worth exploring once you're comfortable with the basics. They require lower hydration and careful handling.

## Hydration adjustments

The recipe here is at 75% hydration — a useful middle ground, workable but with some challenge. Here's what changing it does:

**Lower hydration (65–70%):** Stiffer, denser dough. Much easier to shape and handle. The crumb will be tighter and more uniform — good for sandwich bread where you want even slices.

**Higher hydration (80%+):** Slacker, wetter, harder to handle. Produces the wide-open, irregular crumb you see in ciabatta or high-end bakery loaves. Worth trying once you're confident with shaping, but expect more spreading.

## Enriched variations

Adding fat, dairy, or eggs creates a softer, richer loaf — a different category from lean crusty sourdough, but still wild-leavened.

**Sourdough milk bread / sandwich loaf:** Substitute 50–75% of the water with whole milk. Add 20g of butter incorporated after the initial mix. The result is soft, slightly sweet, and stays fresh longer than a lean loaf. Bake without the Dutch oven, in a loaf pan, at a lower temperature (375°F) for a longer time.

**Olive oil loaf:** Add 20–30g of good olive oil. The crust will be thinner and more tender, the crumb slightly richer. Good for focaccia-style applications or just a loaf that wants less structural drama.

**Sourdough focaccia:** A dramatic hydration jump (85–90%) with generous olive oil in the pan and on top. Dimple it before baking, pile on toppings (cherry tomatoes, flaky salt, rosemary, thinly sliced red onion), and bake flat in a oiled sheet pan. No shaping skill required, very forgiving, and almost impossible to dislike.

## Crust variations

**Seeded crust:** After shaping, brush the surface with water and press into seeds laid out on the counter. Sesame, poppy, or a blend.

**Scoring patterns:** A single ear-slash gives you a dramatic ear and good spring. Grid patterns, leaf designs, and wheat stalks are decorative — score shallower for design work (¼ inch) versus deeper for functional expansion (½ inch).

**Extra-dark crust:** Let it go to deep mahogany. Internal temp well above 205°F. The Maillard reaction keeps going the longer it bakes, and the flavor complexity in a very dark crust is worth it.
