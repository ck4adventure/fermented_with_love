# Feeding and care

A healthy sourdough starter is a surprisingly resilient thing. It can handle irregular schedules, stretches of neglect, and a fair amount of improvisation. What it needs, fundamentally, is flour and water on a regular enough schedule that it doesn't starve.

Once your starter is established, the goal shifts from cultivation to maintenance.

## The flour blend

For ongoing feedings, a **50/50 mix of bread flour and whole wheat flour** hits a good balance. Bread flour (high-protein white flour) gives the starter a clean, neutral base and produces strong gluten structure. Whole wheat contributes bran and germ, which carry trace minerals and wild yeast that keep the culture lively over time.

If you're buying flour rather than milling it, any unbleached bread flour and any whole wheat flour works. You don't need anything specialty.

Pre-mix a batch in a jar and keep it by the starter — it simplifies the feeding routine and removes one more decision.

## The counter routine

If you bake regularly — a few times a week or more — keeping your starter on the counter and feeding it once or twice daily is the easiest approach.

**Basic feeding ratio (1:1:1):**
- 50g starter (discard the rest or use it in something)
- 50g flour blend
- 50g room-temperature water

Stir thoroughly, cover loosely, and leave at room temperature. Your starter will rise, peak, and fall over the next several hours. Feed it again when it's fallen back down, or at least once every 24 hours even if it hasn't.

If you're baking today, feed in the morning and use the starter 4–6 hours later, when it's near its peak.

## Using the fridge

If you're baking once a week or less, the fridge is your friend. Cold slows fermentation dramatically — a refrigerated starter can go 7–10 days between feedings without harm.

**To refrigerate:** Feed the starter as normal, let it sit at room temperature for an hour or two to get the fermentation started, then put it in the fridge. It will continue to ferment slowly and then go dormant.

**To revive it:** Take it out the night before you want to bake. Give it a feeding, leave it at room temperature overnight, and it should be active and ready in the morning. If it's been dormant for more than a week, one feeding might not be enough — give it two feedings over 12–24 hours before using it in a recipe.

A starter that's been in the fridge for a while might have a layer of liquid on top (hooch) and smell quite sharp. Pour off the liquid, discard most of the starter, feed it, and it will come back. It usually takes one to two feedings to return to full activity.

## A few things worth knowing

**You don't need a large starter.** Keeping 100–150g on hand is plenty. Larger volumes just mean more flour to feed it without any real benefit.

**The discard isn't waste.** Every time you feed, you pull off most of the old starter. That discard is active, sour, and full of flavor — good in pancakes, flatbreads, crackers, muffins, or pizza dough. Keep a separate jar of discard in the fridge and add to it over the week.

**Consistency beats perfection.** Feeding the same amount at roughly the same time every day — even if it's not ideal — is better than a chaotic schedule. The culture adapts to whatever rhythm you establish.

**Smell is your gauge.** A healthy, well-fed starter smells yeasty and pleasantly sour. If it smells like vinegar or acetone, it's hungry and over-acidic — feed it more frequently or with a slightly higher flour ratio. If it smells off in a way that seems genuinely wrong (not just funky), trust that instinct.
