# Common additives

If you spend any time reading about home winemaking, you'll encounter a long list of things you can add to your must: nutrients, enzymes, acids, tannins, sulfites, fining agents. It can feel like the implication is that good wine requires a shopping list of interventions.

It doesn't. Particularly with wild fermentation, the baseline philosophy is to let the fruit and the organisms do their work, and to add something only when there's a specific reason to — a problem to solve, a gap to fill, a result you're deliberately trying to achieve. Understanding what each additive does makes it much easier to decide whether you actually need it.

---

## Sulfites (potassium metabisulfite / campden tablets)

**What they do:** Release sulfur dioxide (SO₂) when dissolved, which inhibits or kills wild yeast, bacteria, and oxidative enzymes. Also used as a preservative in finished wine.

**Forms:** Campden tablets (convenient, pre-measured) or potassium metabisulfite powder (more economical for larger batches).

**When conventional winemakers use them:**
- Added to must before fermentation to kill wild organisms and create a blank slate for commercial yeast
- Added to finished wine at racking and bottling to prevent oxidation and microbial spoilage during aging

**For wild fermentation:** Generally avoided before and during fermentation, because they destroy the wild yeast population you're relying on. Some wild fermenters add a small amount at bottling — typically half a campden tablet per gallon — to protect the finished wine during storage. At this stage, fermentation is complete, so you're no longer cultivating anything, and the sulfite acts purely as a preservative.

Whether to use sulfites at bottling is a personal choice. Many wild fruit wines age and store well without them, particularly at 11%+ ABV where alcohol itself does significant preservative work. If you're planning to age a wine for more than a year, or if you've had storage issues in the past, a small campden addition at bottling is reasonable insurance.

## Yeast nutrients

**What they do:** Provide nitrogen and other compounds that yeast need to stay healthy throughout fermentation. The most common are diammonium phosphate (DAP), a direct source of yeast-assimilable nitrogen, and blended products like Fermaid-O or Fermaid-K, which combine DAP with other cofactors including vitamins and minerals.

**Why they matter:** Yeast need more than sugar to thrive — they need nitrogen for building proteins, thiamine for cellular metabolism, and various minerals. Fruit juice, particularly stone fruit and berry juice, can be surprisingly low in yeast-assimilable nitrogen (YAN). Nitrogen-starved yeast produce hydrogen sulfide (that rotten egg smell) as a stress response, and may stall before fermentation is complete.

**For wild fermentation:** Adding nutrients is a reasonable intervention even in an otherwise additive-free process, because you're addressing a nutritional deficit rather than trying to control or replace the organisms doing the work. The wild yeast community benefits from nutrients just as commercial strains do.

If you're going to add nutrients, timing matters. Adding them at the very start, when sugar concentration is highest and yeast are just establishing, is less effective than adding them in stages after fermentation is clearly underway — typically 24–48 hours in and again a few days later. This is called **staggered nutrient addition** and it's standard practice in mead making (where nutrient poverty is even more pronounced) and increasingly common in fruit winemaking.

Signs that your ferment might benefit from nutrients: slow start despite good temperature and healthy-looking fruit, hydrogen sulfide smell during active fermentation, or a history of stuck fermentations with a particular fruit.

## Pectic enzyme (pectinase)

**What it does:** Breaks down pectin, the natural gelling agent found in fruit. In wine, pectin can cause a persistent haze that won't settle out on its own regardless of how long you wait.

**When you need it:** Pectin haze is most common in apple and quince wines, stone fruit wines, and anything made from very ripe or overcooked fruit. It produces a stable, fine cloudiness that looks similar to yeast haze but doesn't respond to racking, cold crashing, or time the way yeast haze does. If your wine has been fully finished and racked multiple times but remains stubbornly hazy, pectin is a likely culprit.

**How to use it:** Added to the must at the beginning of fermentation — before fermentation starts, ideally, since the enzyme works best at lower alcohol levels and higher temperatures. If you're adding it to a finished wine to correct existing haze, it works more slowly and less completely but can still help. Follow the dosage on the package; a small amount goes a long way.

**For wild fermentation:** Pectic enzyme has no effect on yeast or fermentation behavior — it only acts on pectin. Adding it doesn't undermine the wild character of your ferment. It's one of the less controversial additives in the wild fermentation world.

## Acid blend

**What it does:** Increases the acidity of a must or finished wine. Commercial acid blend is typically a mixture of tartaric, malic, and citric acids in proportions designed to approximate the natural acidity of grape must. Individual acids are also available — tartaric acid is the sharpest and most stable, malic acid is softer (and can be consumed by malolactic bacteria during secondary fermentation), citric acid is the most food-flavored of the three and can taste artificial in large amounts.

**Why acidity matters:** Acid plays multiple roles in wine. It contributes to flavor balance (the interplay of sweetness, alcohol, and acidity is what makes a wine taste complete rather than flat or cloying), inhibits many spoilage organisms, and affects color stability in red and rosé wines. Most fruits have enough natural acidity, but some — particularly very ripe stone fruit and tropical fruits — can produce musts that are low in acid and benefit from a small addition.

**How to know if you need it:** Taste the must. A must that tastes flat or overly sweet without any bright edge is often low in acid. You can also test with pH strips or a meter — most fruit wines ferment well in the 3.0–3.5 pH range. Above 3.6 or so, spoilage risk increases and the wine may taste dull.

**For wild fermentation:** Acid additions are neutral in terms of wild yeast impact — the organisms don't particularly care about small pH adjustments within the normal range. Add in small increments and taste as you go. It's much easier to add acid than to remove it.

## Tannin

**What it does:** Tannin is the compound responsible for the dry, slightly astringent sensation you feel in the back of your mouth when you drink a young red wine or strong tea. In wine, it contributes structure, body, and preservative properties — tannin binds to proteins and other compounds, helping the wine clarify and age well.

**Natural sources:** Grape skins, seeds, and stems are naturally high in tannin, which is why red wine has it in abundance. Most fruit wines made without grape products have little to no natural tannin, which can make them feel thin or short on the palate.

**Commercial tannin powder** (available at homebrew shops) can be added to compensate. It's typically derived from oak, grape seeds, or chestnut, and comes in forms with slightly different flavor profiles. A small addition — usually less than half a teaspoon per gallon — can add noticeable body and structure to a fruit wine that tastes flat.

**When it matters:** Tannin is most useful in wines you plan to age for more than a few months, where the structural role of tannin becomes more apparent over time. For a fresh, light fruit wine you're planning to drink within six months, it's less critical. Plum wine specifically benefits from a small tannin addition — plums have some natural tannin in their skins, but not a huge amount, and a little supplementation rounds out the structure of the finished wine.

## Fining agents

Fining agents are added to finished wine to clarify it — they bind to particles in suspension and cause them to clump and drop out. The most common ones you'll encounter:

**Bentonite:** A clay that binds to proteins. The most commonly used fining agent in white and fruit winemaking. Mixed with water to form a slurry and stirred into the wine; proteins and the clay settle together over a few days to a week. Food-grade and vegan.

**Gelatin:** Binds to tannins and fine particulates, works well for red wines or any wine with significant tannin. Not vegan.

**Isinglass:** Derived from fish bladder; very gentle and produces exceptional clarity. Not vegan.

**Sparkolloid:** A blend of polysaccharides and diatomaceous earth; works well for wines that haven't responded to other fining agents.

For most wild fruit wines, patient racking and cold crashing (moving the vessel somewhere near-freezing for a week to encourage particles to clump and settle) will produce good clarity without fining agents. Fining is most useful when you've racked multiple times and the wine simply isn't clearing on its own — the pectin haze situation described above being a common example, or protein haze in a wine made from very ripe fruit.

---

*The consistent thread across all of these is the same: understand what a given additive does, identify whether you actually have the problem it solves, and add as little as you need. Wild fermentation at its best is about working with what's already there — additives are the exception, not the rule.*
