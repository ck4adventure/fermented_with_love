# Sanitation without sulfites

There's a tension at the heart of wild fermentation that's worth naming directly: the organisms you're trying to cultivate — wild yeast living on your fruit and in your environment — are also the kinds of organisms that aggressive sanitization is designed to kill. If you sterilize everything in pursuit of a clean ferment, you may inadvertently eliminate the very thing that makes wild fermentation work.

This doesn't mean cleanliness doesn't matter. It absolutely does. But the goal is different from what commercial winemaking manuals typically describe. You're not trying to create a sterile environment. You're trying to create a *clean* one — free of dirt, residue, and contaminants that would cause problems, while still hospitable to the organisms you actually want.

Sandor Katz, whose writing on fermentation has shaped a lot of how I think about this, frames it simply: soap and hot water, thoroughly rinsed. That's the baseline, and in practice it's enough.

## The difference between clean and sterile

**Clean** means free of visible dirt, residue, and the kinds of organic material (fruit pulp, sugar residue, old wine) that would feed harmful organisms or introduce off-flavors. This is what soap and water achieves.

**Sterile** means free of all living microorganisms — the goal of a hospital operating room, and also the goal of conventional commercial winemaking, where a blank slate is created and then inoculated with a specific commercial yeast.

For wild fermentation, sterile is not only unnecessary — it's counterproductive. You *want* organisms present. The distinction you're making is not between sterile and non-sterile but between a clean environment where your target organisms can thrive and a contaminated one where the wrong organisms get a foothold first.

## The practical approach: soap, hot water, thorough rinsing

Wash all equipment — fermentation vessels, airlocks, bungs, spoons, siphon tubing, bottles — with unscented dish soap and hot water before and after each use. Scrub any visible residue. Then rinse *thoroughly*. Soap residue inhibits fermentation and can affect flavor, so rinse until the vessel smells like nothing at all, then rinse once more.

Hot water alone (without soap) is fine for rinsing between uses during an active fermentation — for example, rinsing a stirring spoon before returning it to the vessel.

Let equipment air dry rather than towel drying where possible. Towels can introduce lint and occasionally harbor organisms you don't want.

That's genuinely all you need for most situations. The active fermentation environment — once it's going — is quite hostile to most unwanted organisms. The low pH and rising alcohol of an active ferment do a lot of protective work on their own.

## Where extra care matters most

A few situations where you'll want to be particularly attentive:

**Bottles before bottling.** Bottles that have been sitting around, even clean ones, can accumulate dust and the occasional airborne mold spore. Rinse thoroughly with hot water immediately before filling, invert to drain, and fill while still slightly warm.

**Siphon tubing.** Flexible tubing is harder to clean than smooth glass or plastic because the inside can develop biofilm over time. Run hot soapy water through the tubing after each use, then rinse. Replace tubing if it develops a persistent smell that washing doesn't remove.

**Airlocks.** The water inside the airlock is in contact with your wine vapor and can become a place for things to grow if left unchanged for long periods. Refresh the water in your airlock every few weeks during a long secondary fermentation.

**Any equipment that touched a batch with off-flavors.** If a vessel held wine that developed vinegar character or other notable problems, wash it especially carefully — ideally with a hot water soak after the soap wash — before using it for a new batch.

## Chemical sanitizers: what they are and when they make sense

The most commonly used chemical sanitizer in home winemaking is **Star San**, a phosphoric acid-based product that's diluted in water and used as a no-rinse sanitizer. You mix it at about one ounce per five gallons of water, rinse your equipment with the solution, let it drain without rinsing (the remaining foam is harmless and breaks down quickly), and proceed. It's effective, food-safe at working concentration, and widely available at homebrew shops.

The reason conventional winemaking recommends it is the same reason it's not necessarily the right tool for wild fermentation: it kills essentially everything on contact. Used before inoculating with commercial yeast, that's exactly what you want — a blank slate. Used when you're hoping to preserve wild yeast populations on your fruit and in your environment, it works against you.

That said, there are situations where chemical sanitization makes sense even for wild fermenters:

- If a batch has gone seriously wrong — real vinegar, strong off-flavors, visible contamination — and you want to be sure the next batch starts clean
- For equipment that's difficult to fully clean mechanically (long tubing, narrow-necked vessels)
- If you're using commercial yeast for a specific batch and want to give it the best possible start

**Campden tablets** (potassium metabisulfite) are worth a brief mention here because you'll encounter them in almost every conventional winemaking resource. Dissolved in water or added directly to must, they release sulfur dioxide, which kills or inhibits wild yeast and bacteria. They're used to sterilize must before adding commercial yeast, and to preserve finished wine during aging and bottling.

For wild fermentation, campden tablets are generally avoided precisely because of what they do. Adding them to your must would kill the wild yeast you're counting on. Some wild fermenters use a small amount at bottling to help preserve the finished wine — at that stage, fermentation is complete and you're no longer trying to cultivate anything. But this is optional, and many wild-fermented fruit wines bottle and age perfectly well without any sulfite additions.

## A note on equipment materials

Glass and food-grade plastic clean up well and don't harbor organisms in their surfaces under normal use. Stainless steel is excellent and easy to sanitize. Avoid wooden implements for stirring or any wood in contact with must or wine — wood is porous and can harbor organisms and flavor compounds from previous batches in ways that are very difficult to clean out. (The exception is intentional oak aging with commercial oak alternatives, which is a different and controlled situation.)
